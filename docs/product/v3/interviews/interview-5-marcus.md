# Interview: Marcus T., 28
*Multi-instrumentalist studio musician, Austin TX — banjo/guitar/cavaquinho teacher (Zoom + in-person), part-time barista, gig musician. The Skeptic / Acquired-and-Lost persona.*

*Date: 2026-05-20*

---

## Pull quotes

> "I rage-quit. And here's the thing — I almost never rage-quit. I'm 28, I work in DAWs, I expect software to be janky. But that moment, where the banner shoves the layout up and my thumb taps an ad while my D string is still ringing — that's not a bug, that's a worldview. Somebody at Banjen decided that ad impression was worth more than the tuning. So I decided Banjen wasn't worth the install."

> "LikeTones isn't great. It's fine. But 'fine and no ads' beats 'great and ads' every single time. That's not a preference, that's math."

> "If you fix the ad placement tomorrow, I might give you another shot — but only because the core idea is actually right. Looping reference tones, no mic permission, no signup — that is the correct architecture. You just monetized the one screen you were not allowed to monetize."

---

**Q1:** Walk me through the last time you opened Banjen — before you deleted it.

**A1:** OK so this was — what, late January? I was at Rafa's home studio, we were tracking a choro arrangement, cavaquinho and banjo both in DGBD. My Boss TU-3 was wired up for the electric and I didn't feel like re-patching, so I figured I'd just use my phone. Someone on r/banjo had mentioned Banjen as the "just hit a button and hear the note" app, and that's exactly what I wanted because GStrings makes me dig through three menus every time. So I installed it, opened it — four big buttons, D G B D, looping playback. Honestly? My first reaction was "this is perfect." No mic permission popup, no signup, no onboarding carousel. I tapped the D, the tone started, and I picked up my banjo. For about eleven seconds I was a fan.

**Q2:** What specifically made you delete it? Be exact.

**A2:** Exact: I tapped the D string, the tone started playing, I had my banjo up against my chest, my left hand on the peg, my right hand holding the phone. I'm listening, comparing, turning the peg slowly. Then a banner ad loaded in at the bottom of the screen — and the whole layout shifted up to make room for it. The string buttons moved. My thumb was already in motion to tap the D again to re-hear it, and instead my thumb landed on the ad. Chrome opens. Some random landing page — I think it was a gambling thing, or maybe a game install, I genuinely don't remember because I was already done. My banjo string is still ringing behind the Chrome window. I switched back, uninstalled, went to GStrings, finished the session. That was it. One session. One ad. Permanent delete. And before you ask — no, I didn't write a review, because I was on the clock and Rafa was waiting.

**Q3:** What do you use now? Why did it earn the install?

**A3:** For acoustic, GStrings most of the time, LikeTones sometimes. GStrings because it's free, it works, it doesn't try to sell me anything mid-tune. The UX is honestly mediocre — chromatic mode is buried, the needle is twitchy in any room with HVAC noise — but it doesn't disrespect me. LikeTones I installed after I saw their tagline: "No Ads, No Purchases, No Registration." That's basically a manifesto. The app itself? Eh. The reference tones are fine, the interface is plain, it's not winning any design awards. But the *promise* is the product. They told me what they wouldn't do, and they've kept the promise. For electric I just use the Boss TU-3 pedal — hundred bucks, lasts forever, never updates itself, never asks me to log in.

**Q4:** How does your daily tuner workflow look? How often, where, what device?

**A4:** I tune probably eight to fifteen times a day depending. Morning practice — guitar, maybe banjo. Afternoon Zoom lessons — three to five students, each one tunes at the start, I help them tune over video. Evening session or recording — usually two or three instruments. Pixel 8 is my phone. At home I've got the Boss TU-3 on a pedalboard for plug-in stuff. The friction for me isn't accuracy — accuracy is solved, it's been solved since the 90s. The friction is context-switching. I might tune a guitar in EADGBE, then a banjo in DGBD, then a cavaquinho in DGBD, then back to guitar. Every menu dive is creative momentum I don't get back. That's why Banjen's button-per-string idea was so good — and that's why the ad thing hurt so much. The architecture was right.

**Q5:** What would Banjen need to do to earn a re-install?

**A5:** Three conditions, non-negotiable. One — no ads of any kind on the tuning screen. Not a banner, not a "swipe up for more," not a sponsored string button. The tuning surface is sacred. Monetize literally anywhere else — splash screen, settings, a "tuned!" confirmation dialog, an interstitial when I exit the app. I do not care. Two — a homescreen widget. Tap the widget, hear the D. That's the workflow I actually want. Three — adjustable reference pitch, A=432 through A=446. I record with a couple of artists who insist on 432, and right now I have to generate reference tones in Ableton because no Banjen-class app lets me shift the reference. Hit all three, I reinstall the day you ship it. Miss any one, I'm staying on GStrings.

**Q6:** Tell me about students — what do beginners get wrong about tuning?

**A6:** Almost everything, but in a predictable order. First — they don't know what direction to turn the peg, so they pull the string up half a step when they meant to come down a quarter, then they panic. Second — they think "in tune" is a binary, like the app says green so we're done. They don't develop the ear, they develop the habit of looking at the phone. That's a long-term loss. Third, and this is the one I actually care about — beginners tune to the wrong octave. They tune the low D up to a D4 because the app's needle locks onto the nearest pitch class. Reference-tone apps don't have that problem because you're matching the actual note you hear. Which, by the way, is one of the few things Banjen got right philosophically. Ear-training-by-stealth. That's an actual pedagogical win.

**Q7:** What's your hot take on the chromatic-vs-reference-tone debate?

**A7:** It's not a debate, it's a workflow question and people keep treating it like a religion. Chromatic is faster when you know what you're doing and the room is quiet — you see the needle, you adjust, you move on. Reference tone is better when the room is loud, when the student doesn't know what note they're aiming for, when you want to train the ear instead of train the eye, or when you're tuning a non-standard instrument like a cavaquinho and the chromatic tuner doesn't know your tuning. The correct product has both modes and lets you switch in one tap. Banjen made the bet that reference-only is enough, and for their primary user — old dudes tuning DGBD in their kitchen — they're probably right. For me, multi-instrument, multi-context, they need both eventually. But not before they fix the ads.

**Q8:** If Banjen added cavaquinho support, would you care?

**A8:** I'd care a lot, actually, and here's the funny thing — they already have it and don't know it. DGBD is DGBD. The strings on a cavaquinho are tuned to the same pitches Banjen already plays. They literally just need to put the word "cavaquinho" in the Play Store listing and in a settings label and they would unlock — what, a million Brazilian players? Rafa would install it tomorrow. My Austin Brazilian music group, that's maybe twenty people, eighteen of them would install it. It's the cheapest market expansion in app history because the code already works. Whoever's running their growth strategy and hasn't shipped a Portuguese listing yet should be losing sleep.

**Q9:** If they fixed the ad placement but kept the banner, is that enough?

**A9:** Depends entirely on where the banner is and whether it causes layout shift. If it's at the bottom, fixed-position, never animates in or out, never causes the string buttons to move — and it's there from the moment I open the screen so my muscle memory knows where everything is — fine. I'll tolerate that. That's the model GStrings uses, more or less, and I haven't uninstalled GStrings. What I will not tolerate is a banner that loads in late, after the tuning screen is already drawn, and shifts the UI to make room. That's the specific failure that killed Banjen for me. It's also a Core Web Vitals concept — Cumulative Layout Shift — and any web dev with a year of experience knows you reserve the ad slot at render time. Whoever built Banjen's ad integration skipped that lesson.

**Q10:** Would you pay $5 for ad-free + alt tunings? $10?

**A10:** Five bucks, one-time, ad-free plus alt tunings? Yeah, instantly. That's literally a third of a pedal patch cable, I'd buy it without thinking. Ten bucks I'd hesitate, not because ten bucks is a lot but because the comparison point isn't other apps — it's a twelve-dollar Snark clip-on that I can leave on the headstock and never charge. Hardware sets the price ceiling for software in this category, and most app developers don't seem to get that. Subscription? Absolutely not, ever, full stop. If I see "$2.99/month" I close the App Store page. I record with people who pay $60/month for Pro Tools — they will not pay $2.99/month for a tuner. It's a category thing.

**Q11:** What's a competitive feature you wish someone built?

**A11:** Homescreen widget. I have said this to every tuner app I've ever used and nobody ships it. I want a 2x2 widget on my Pixel home screen with four buttons — D, G, B, D — that play the tone instantly without opening the app. That's the actual workflow. Opening an app to hear a single tone is two extra taps and a launch animation. With a widget, my tuning surface is just on the home screen at all times. The second one is multi-instrument profiles — I switch between guitar, banjo, cavaquinho during a single session, and I want a saved profile for each so I'm not picking notes from a master list every time. Third — and this is niche — A=432Hz support. I know it's a tiny market, but the people who care, *really* care.

**Q12:** How do you actually evaluate a new tuner app — what's your test?

**A12:** I have a five-minute test. First thirty seconds — does it ask for mic permission before I've tapped anything? If yes, uninstall, that's a privacy red flag for a tuner that should at least let me try reference tone first. Next minute — does the tuning screen have ads, and do they cause layout shift? That's the killer. Next — how many taps to get a D playing? More than two, that's a fail. Then I pick up a banjo or guitar and actually tune. If I can tune all four strings in under sixty seconds without context-switching, it passes. Final step — I look at the settings screen. If it's full of upsell prompts and "unlock premium" badges, I uninstall. The settings screen tells you what the developer actually cares about.

**Q13:** Where do you discover apps? App Store, Reddit, students, YouTube?

**A13:** Reddit primarily — r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, r/banjo occasionally, r/edmproduction for production stuff. The Play Store search is basically useless because the top results are all sponsored or have fake review counts. YouTube gear reviews sometimes, but I trust the Reddit threads more because they're not monetized — usually. Friends at gigs, definitely — Rafa told me about a metronome app last month that's genuinely good. Students recommend stuff sometimes, more often I'm recommending stuff to them. App Store editorial features? Never. The Apple/Google editorial picks are not for working musicians, they're for casual users. I don't think I've ever installed an app because I saw it on a Play Store homepage.

**Q14:** Have you ever recommended a tuner app on your channel? Outcome?

**A14:** I've got a small YouTube channel — like 2,800 subs, mostly banjo and cavaquinho tutorials. I mentioned GStrings in a video about home recording setup, maybe eighteen months ago. I got two or three comments thanking me. Nothing viral. I would *love* to recommend Banjen — like genuinely, the reference-tone-first concept is so clearly the right idea for beginners, and beginners are my audience. I almost recorded a "best tuner apps for banjo" video last fall and Banjen was going to be in it. After the ad shift thing I shelved the video because I didn't want to put my name on something that would make my viewers tap an ad and rage at me. That's the audience-trust cost of bad UX, by the way — you don't just lose the user, you lose every user that user could have brought you.

**Q15:** What's the dumbest UX mistake tuner apps make again and again?

**A15:** Putting ads on the tuning screen. It's so dumb, it's so universal, and it's so obviously self-defeating that I genuinely don't understand how it keeps happening. The tuning screen is the moment of maximum task focus and minimum tolerance for interruption. Tuning is a precision activity — you're listening, you're making fine adjustments, you're at the edge of your perceptual ability. An ad in that context isn't an ad, it's an attack. The second dumbest mistake is requiring microphone permission for an app that hasn't told the user what it's going to do with the mic. Third — and this is GuitarTuna's signature move — hiding the banjo behind a paywall when banjo is in the app name's search results. That's a bait-and-switch and people remember.

**Q16:** Does microphone permission matter to you? Your students?

**A16:** Matters more to my students than to me, honestly. I'm tech-comfortable, I've granted mic permission to a hundred apps, I know the OS sandbox is real. But my older students — I teach a guy named Bill, he's 64, retired electrical engineer, sharp as a tack — Bill genuinely will not install an app that asks for mic permission unprompted. He thinks Facebook is listening to him through the phone, which is wrong technically but emotionally he's not wrong about the surveillance vibe. The reference-tone approach completely sidesteps that whole conversation. You don't ask for permission you don't need. Banjen got this right and they should put it on the listing page in big letters. "No microphone required." That's a feature.

**Q17:** If Banjen added GDAE preset, would that move you?

**A17:** Move me personally? No, I don't play tenor banjo, I don't do Irish trad. But would it move my opinion of Banjen as a *product*? Yeah, because it would tell me the team finally understands that DGBD is not the only tuning that matters. Right now Banjen is one tuning deep — it's a single-purpose app, which is fine for Harold, but it's a ceiling. Add GDAE, add CGBD, add a couple of cavaquinho regional variants, and now you're a real four-string-instrument tuner with category leadership potential instead of a DGBD novelty. The reference-tone-first architecture scales to all of those tunings trivially. They just need to ship the tuning picker UI without screwing it up.

**Q18:** Have you ever DM'd an app developer? What happened?

**A18:** Once. A metronome app called Tempo had a bug where the subdivision setting reset every time you backgrounded the app. I emailed the dev — small studio, like two people — and they replied in like four hours, fixed it in the next update, even credited me in the release notes. I bought their pro version that day. That's the model. Indie developers who respond to users build absurd loyalty. The flip side — I tweeted at GuitarTuna about the banjo paywall thing and got an automated "thanks for your feedback" response. Never opened that app again. The lesson is: small developers can punch way above their weight if they just talk to their users. I suspect Banjen is a small team. They should be DMing me, not the other way around.

**Q19:** If you were running Banjen tomorrow, what's the first thing you do?

**A19:** Move the ad off the tuning screen. Today. Before lunch. Ship the build, push it to Play Store, do the emergency review. Everything else — alt tunings, widgets, A=432, cavaquinho ASO — can wait a week. The ad on the tuning screen is bleeding users every minute. Every install that doesn't convert is somebody who will tell their banjo teacher "I tried that one, don't bother." That word-of-mouth damage compounds. Second thing — and this is the same day if I can — I'd push a Play Store listing update with "cavaquinho," "afinador," "cavaco," "samba" in the keywords and a translated Portuguese description. That's a one-afternoon job that probably triples your downloads in a month. Third thing — I'd hire one usability tester aged 65+ and watch them use the app for thirty minutes and take notes.

**Q20:** One sentence — the most useful thing the team could hear.

**A20:** You built the correct app and then monetized the one square inch you weren't allowed to touch — fix that and you have a real product; leave it alone and LikeTones takes your market while you're A/B testing banner placements.
