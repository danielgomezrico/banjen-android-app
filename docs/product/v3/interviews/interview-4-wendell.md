# Interview: Wendell P., 71

*Retired Dixieland-band plectrum-banjo player in New Orleans, CGBD tuning, arthritic hands, tech 1/5. Wife Edna passed eighteen months ago; daughter Camille manages his phone.*

**Date:** 2026-05-20

---

## Pull Quotes

> "Edna paid the bills on that thing. She knew where everything was. I just press the green button when somebody calls."

> "The boys don't care if it takes me ten minutes. But I care. I'm the one sittin' there with my hands shakin' and Marcus lookin' over the top of the piano like, you alright, Wendell?"

> "Don't make me read. That's all I'm askin'. Don't make me read little words on a little screen with my hands tremblin'. Make a sound. I'll find it."

---

## Q&A

**Q1: Tell me about getting back into playing after Edna passed.**

**A1:** Well... it wasn't quick. Edna went in October, and I didn't open the case till after Mardi Gras. Didn't feel right. The banjo was always somethin' I did Tuesday nights at rehearsal — she'd be home with her stories, I'd come back smellin' like the bar, she'd say *you smell like Storyville*, even though Storyville's been gone since before either of us was born. Just her joke. Anyway. Robert from the band, the trombone, he came by in February and just sat on the porch and didn't say nothin' for a while, and then said *Wendell, we miss the four-string*. That was it. I went and got it out. The strings were a mess but my hands remembered. They remembered before I did, you understand? That was the strange part. The hands knew before the head caught up.

**Q2: Walk me through the last time you tried to tune your banjo.**

**A2:** Last time? That was... Tuesday. Yeah, Tuesday before rehearsal. I picked it up around ten in the mornin' 'cause that's when the medicine for my hands gets workin' good. Strummed across, and the second string sounded sour. Now, forty years I know what sour sounds like. I just need *one note* to hang the rest off of. So I picked up the phone, and... I sat there. I sat there with the phone in my lap for I don't know how long. 'Cause last time I tried one of those tuner things I tapped somethin' I shouldn't have, and Camille had to come over Saturday and *clean it up*, she called it. I don't know what got downloaded. She wasn't mad but she had that voice. So I just put the phone down and played the banjo a little sharp and went on with my mornin'. That's the truth of it.

**Q3: What tuning do you use? Why?**

**A3:** C-G-B-D. Plectrum tuning. That's what we played comin' up — Dixieland, the old four-string, that's what it is. Now everybody and they brother on YouTube's got a five-string banjo with that high drone on it and they call that banjo. That ain't *my* banjo. My banjo's got four strings and the low one is a C. Now look — I know the middle three, the G the B and the D, those are the same as what most banjos do. So I figure these tuner apps oughta get me three outta four. But that low C, nobody plays it. I gotta find that one off the piano. Marcus, the piano player at rehearsal, he hits a C for me. That's how I get my C.

**Q4: What does the app look like in your hands? Buttons, colors, sounds?**

**A4:** Hm. Well — I'm thinkin' about it now, what I'd *want* to see, you understand, not what I've actually seen 'cause I don't really see it, I just close it. But — big. Big buttons. My hands shake some, and the screen's small, and the print is little. Camille keeps tellin' me to make the print bigger and I keep forgettin' how. So big squares. And the *sound* — I want the sound to come out warm, like a real note. Not a *beep*. A note, like somebody played it on a real instrument. Colors? I don't know. Don't make it too bright, my eyes water in the mornin' before the drops kick in. That's all I got on colors. The boys would laugh at me bein' asked about colors.

**Q5: Has Camille ever had to fix your phone after you tried to tune?**

**A5:** Oh yes. Yeah. That was the time I'm tellin' you about — the free one I downloaded, I don't even remember what it was called. I touched somethin' on the screen, and the next thing it's askin' me about a subscription, and then somethin' else opened up, and I panicked. I called Camille that night. She came Saturday — she lives over by the lake — and she sat with me at the kitchen table and went through everything on the phone and uninstalled a couple things and said *Daddy, don't put any more music apps on here without callin' me first*. And I haven't. That was — what — a year ago? More? I haven't tried again. She wasn't ugly about it but she was *worried*, and I don't like bein' the thing my daughter worries about. I'm supposed to be the one worryin' about her.

**Q6: What other apps do you use on your phone?**

**A6:** The green one for callin'. The blue one for the messages — though I mostly just read 'em, Camille writes me a lot. The weather. Sometimes the weather. There's a... a thing for the pharmacy refills, my prescriptions, Camille set that up but I don't open it, I just call the pharmacy. Edna, she had the apps for everything. The bank, the bills, the airline when we went to see her sister in Atlanta. She knew where everything was. I just press the green button when somebody calls. That's about the size of it. Oh — and the camera. Camille showed me the camera, for if I wanted to send her a picture of the garden. I sent her one picture, of a tomato. That was a year ago.

**Q7: What's hard about your hands and a small touchscreen?**

**A7:** Everything. (laughs) Everything's hard. My fingers don't bend like they used to — the arthritis, it's in the knuckles mostly, the right hand worse than the left. So when I go to tap somethin' on that little screen, my finger comes down kinda flat, like the whole pad of it, and I hit *two* things at once, or I hit the thing next to what I meant. And then sometimes — and this is the part I don't tell Camille — sometimes my hand shakes a little. Just a little. Mostly when I'm tired or it's cold. So I'm aimin' at one thing and my finger lands somewhere else. The buttons need to be big and they need *space* between 'em. If they're crammed up together like little teeth, I'm just gonna miss. I'll miss every time.

**Q8: Have you ever paid for an app? Why or why not?**

**A8:** No. No, I have not. Now Edna paid for things on the phone, on her phone — she had the puzzle one, the word puzzle, she paid somethin' once a year for that, she liked it. But I never have. And I'll tell you why — 'cause I don't know how, for one. I don't know where my card number's at on this phone. And 'cause I'm scared of payin' for somethin' I didn't mean to pay for. You hear about that. The grandkids — Camille's boys — they tell stories about people tappin' a wrong thing and gettin' charged every month forever. That's the kinda thing puts a knot in my stomach. So I just don't.

**Q9: When an ad blocks the screen, what happens?**

**A9:** I close the phone. (laughs) I mean it. I push that side button, the screen goes black, and I set it on the table. 'Cause if somethin' big comes up on the screen that I didn't ask for, my first thought is *don't touch nothin', Wendell, don't make it worse*. So I just kill the screen and walk away. Now sometimes there's a little X, and you're supposed to find the X, and I've heard Darnell — I mean Camille's husband — I've heard him talk about how the X moves around or it's tiny on purpose. That's not for me. I'm not playin' that game. I just put the phone down. And whatever I was tryin' to do, I'm not doin' it anymore. That's the end of it.

**Q10: Where do you practice — what does the room look like, sound like?**

**A10:** Back bedroom. We had a spare room — was gonna be a sewin' room for Edna but she never did take it up — so it's got a window faces the side yard, a chair, a little table for my coffee, and a stand for the banjo. Light comes in pretty good in the mornin'. Quiet. I can hear the air conditioner if it's runnin'. Sometimes a dog two doors down. That's it. I sit in the chair, the banjo on my knee, and I play for fifteen, twenty minutes till my hands say no more. The phone goes on the table next to the coffee. If I gotta look at it I gotta put on my readin' glasses, which means leanin' over and pickin' 'em up off the table, and by then I've usually already given up on the phone.

**Q11: What's the band like — how does tuning happen there?**

**A11:** Oh, the band, the band's wonderful. We rehearse Tuesday nights at a hall over by Treme, six of us — Marcus on piano, Robert the trombone, Big Eddie on the clarinet, James on the bass, our drummer Lou, and me on the four-string. Some of 'em been playin' together since the seventies. Now tuning — here's how it works. I get there twenty minutes early. Always. I call it the early-bird tuning club, 'cause Robert does the same. We come early, Marcus is already at the piano warmin' up, and he plays the notes for us, one at a time. C, G, B, D. I tune off the piano. Five minutes, I'm done. Now if I had somethin' on the phone could give me those notes at *home*, before I left the house, I wouldn't have to be the early-bird anymore. I could be... regular bird. Whatever that is.

**Q12: Does playing music help with anything else in your life?**

**A12:** ...Yeah. Yeah it does. I don't talk about this much. After Edna — after she went — I had a stretch there where I wasn't gettin' out the house. My doctor, she's a young lady, she asked me some questions one visit and she said *Mr. P., you might be a little blue right now, that's normal, but we wanna keep an eye on it*. And she said *what do you do that gets you out the house*. And I said *Tuesday night I play with the boys*. And she said *good, you keep doin' that, that's medicine*. Those were her words. *That's medicine.* So when I can't tune the banjo, it's not just I can't tune the banjo. It's the medicine's not workin'. You see what I'm sayin'? If I miss too many Tuesdays — that's a road I don't wanna look down.

**Q13: What worried you about getting older and still playing?**

**A13:** Everything worries me about it. (pause) The hands, mostly. I watch my hands and they're not the same hands. Used to be I could play the wire-string-on-wire-frets thing for two hours at a stretch and now twenty minutes and I'm done. So I think about — when does twenty become fifteen, and fifteen become ten, and ten become *I can't*. The eyes, too. The little print on the music stand, I can't see it the way I used to. Things move on me. And then the phone — the phone's the worst, 'cause it changes. Camille tells me they *update* it, and one day the buttons are in one place and the next week they moved. Why would they do that? Who'd a thought that was a good idea? An old man tryin' to find the green button and they moved it. That's worried me more than I let on.

**Q14: What does "in tune" mean to you, after sixty years?**

**A14:** It means the chord sits down. That's what I always told my nephew when he was startin' — I said *boy, when it's in tune the chord sits down, when it's outta tune the chord stands up*. You can hear it. It's not a number on a screen, it's not a green light, it's a thing that *settles*. The four strings, when they're right, they ring together like one voice, and when they're wrong one of 'em is stickin' out, talkin' over the others. After sixty years I can hear which one's stickin' out, usually, but I still need a *note* to chase. Just one good note. The rest I can do. That's why I keep tellin' you — give me one note I trust, and I'm gone. I don't need a needle. Needles are for people who can't hear.

**Q15: Have you ever asked Camille for help with a tuner app?**

**A15:** No. No, I haven't asked her. And I'll tell you why — 'cause after that mess with the one I downloaded, where she had to come clean it up, I don't wanna be the daddy who keeps callin' the daughter to fix things. She's got two boys and a job and a husband, and she comes by on Sundays for dinner, and I don't wanna be a *task* on her list. So when somethin' on the phone breaks or I don't know how to use it, I just... don't use it. I work around. The band, the piano, the early-bird thing. Now — if she handed me a phone and said *Daddy, this one's all set up, just press these buttons*, that'd be different. That I could do. But me callin' her sayin' *how do I get the tuner app*? No. I'm not doin' that.

**Q16: Microphone permission — does that mean anything to you?**

**A16:** A what permission? Microphone? (long pause) I... no. I mean, I know what a microphone is, but I don't know what *permission* means on a phone. Is that the thing where it asks you somethin' and there's a box with a yes and a no? 'Cause if it does that, I press the no. I press the no every time. Don't matter what it's askin'. 'Cause Camille told me, when in doubt say no, *Daddy don't agree to anything you didn't go lookin' for*. So whatever the microphone is wantin', it ain't gettin' it from me. Now you tellin' me a tuner needs the microphone to work? Then that tuner ain't workin', not on my phone. Make one that don't need to listen.

**Q17: If the app cost three dollars to remove ads, would you pay?**

**A17:** Three dollars... Hm. Now, three dollars is nothin'. That's less than a beer, that's less than a po-boy. So in terms of *would the money matter*, no. I wouldn't blink at three dollars. The question is, can I *do it*. Can I get the three dollars from my pocket to the phone without somethin' goin' sideways. 'Cause that's where I get nervous — not at the price, at the *transaction*. If Camille was here and she could push the buttons for me, sure. Three dollars, ten dollars, twenty dollars even, if the thing worked. But me doin' it alone? I'd sit there with my finger hoverin' over the button and put it down. I'd have to ask Camille. Which goes back to what I said before.

**Q18: If Banjen disappeared, what would you do?**

**A18:** ...Well, son, I never had Banjen, so it wouldn't *disappear* from me. I'd be in the same place I'm in now, which is — early-bird tuning club, Marcus on the piano, twenty minutes early to Tuesday rehearsal, and if I wanna play at home in the mornin' I play a little outta tune and live with it. So nothin' changes for me. Now if you tellin' me I *had* Banjen and was usin' it every day and then it went away — that'd hurt. 'Cause it'd mean I'd had somethin' that worked, and I learned to lean on it, and then they took it. I'd rather not have it at all than have it taken. That's somethin' you get to feelin' at my age. Don't give me somethin' you can't promise to keep.

**Q19: What does a tuner app need that no app has gotten right?**

**A19:** Big buttons. Big buttons that make a *sound*. That's the thing. Make a button the size of my thumb, paint it a clear color, put the letter on it big enough I don't need my readin' glasses, and when I press it, a *banjo* note comes out — not a beep, not a buzz, a banjo. And keep playin' the note. Don't stop after a second. 'Cause I gotta hold the banjo and turn the peg, I can't be pressin' the phone every two seconds. Let it play. Let it play till I press it again. And — I'm gonna say this twice 'cause it matters — *don't put anything on the screen I can accidentally touch that's gonna charge me money or open up somethin' I didn't ask for*. That's the whole thing. Big buttons. Banjo notes. Nothin' I can hurt myself with. That's the app. Somebody needs to build that and stop buildin' everything else.

**Q20: One sentence to the people who make Banjen.**

**A20:** Make it so an old man with shakin' hands and bad eyes and a dead wife can press one big button in his back bedroom on a Tuesday mornin' and hear his banjo's note come out clear, and don't put nothin' on that screen that's gonna scare him off — 'cause if you do that, I promise you, the boys in the band'll hear about it, and so will the doctor who told me music's medicine, and that's a longer reach than you'd think for a man who don't go on the internet.
