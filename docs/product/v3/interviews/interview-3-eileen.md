## Interview

**Q1: Walk me through the last session and what happened with tuning.**
**A1:** Tuesday gone, Hughes'. I got in at half-six, fifteen before the start, which is standard. The place was already roaring — there was a match on the telly above the bar and the radiator off the back wall has a hiss on it you wouldn't believe. I dug out the A fork, struck it on me knee, got the A sorted, then derived D off it, then the G, then the high E. Or that was the plan. By the time I'd worked down to the G I couldn't tell if it was right because two fellas at the next table were having a proper row about Mayo football. Sat the first reel out like a lemon, adjusted between the first and second tune, and was second-guessing the G for half the night. That's a normal Tuesday, by the way. That's not a bad Tuesday.

**Q2: What do you do when a beginner sits in and their banjo is out of tune?**
**A2:** Depends on the beginner, and depends on how badly out. If they're a quarter-tone off and they're nervous, I'll just play through it and have a quiet word at the break — nobody learns anything from being publicly corrected mid-set, and trad has enough gatekeeping without me adding to it. If they're properly out — like, dog-roaring out — I'll lean over, say "give us your A there a sec", strike me fork against the table, and we'll sort it together in thirty seconds. The ones I've no patience for are the lads who arrive *knowing* they're out and just sit in anyway hoping nobody'll notice. We notice. The box player notices first and he's not subtle about the face he pulls.

**Q3: Show me how you tune. Talk through every step.**
**A3:** Right. Case open on me lap or on a chair. Banjo across the knee. Fork lives in the little zip pocket on the inside of the lid — supposedly. Half the time it's slid through the lining and is rattling around inside the case body, so step one is often a thirty-second forage. Strike the fork against my kneecap — never against the banjo, you'd be amazed how many beginners do that — bring it to the ear, sing the A back to meself, then play the second string and match it. From the A I drop a fifth to the D, drop another fifth to the G — that's the troublesome one, the low G, fat string, slow to settle — then up a fifth from the A to the high E. Whole thing's maybe forty seconds in a quiet room. In Hughes' it can be three minutes if the football's on. Then I check it across two strings together, double-stop style, listen for the beats. That's the bit a needle can't do for you.

**Q4: Have you used a tuner app? Which? What annoyed you?**
**A4:** I've tried a rake of them. Cleartune was the one I lived with longest — works grand at home in the kitchen, useless in a pub the second the place fills up. The mic just hoovers in everything. PitchLab I gave a fair go — looks like the cockpit of a 747, I'm tuning a banjo not landing at Shannon. GuitarTuna wants me to make an account and pay a subscription for what was a free feature five years ago, so that's a no from me. I tried a few generic chromatics — they'll tell you you're playing an A♭ when what you want is for them to *just give you an A*. The big annoyance across all of them is they assume I'm a learner who doesn't know what note I want. I know what note I want. I want the note.

**Q5: What about pitch pipes, clip-ons, electronic tuners on the stand?**
**A5:** Pitch pipes are grand in theory, fierce hard to blow cleanly when you've a pint in you. Clip-ons — I've a Snark somewhere, a yellow yoke. The vibration crosstalk in a pub is a nightmare; you've five people on stringed instruments at the one table and your clip-on's picking up the box player's bellows through the floorboards. Also I just think it looks daft, a wee thing perched on the headstock like a budgie. The stand tuners are for guitar players doing soundchecks, not for trad. The fork is what works. The fork's just feckin' losable.

**Q6: If a tuner app only did DGBD and not GDAE, would you use it?**
**A6:** No. Next question. (Pause.) Sorry, you wanted more than that. Look — DGBD is a 4-string banjo tuning, but it's the *Chicago* tuning, the plectrum and the cavaquinho tuning. It is not Irish tenor. Irish tenor is GDAE because we play with fiddles and mandolins and we want the low G and we want the fingering to map to the mandolin notation. If your app opens to D-G-B-D and there's no obvious way to switch, the conversation's over before it started. I've closed the tab. I'm gone. And I'll have told the WhatsApp not to bother as well.

**Q7: What's the smallest change that would make you actively recommend Banjen?**
**A7:** GDAE as a first-class preset on the home screen. Not buried three menus deep under "alternative tunings" like it's some exotic curiosity. The first thing I see when I open the app should be a tuning picker — DGBD, GDAE, CGBD, whatever — and the second thing should be four big buttons that play the actual notes. Reference tones, looping, no needle, no mic. If you ship that with GDAE on the front page I will post about it on TheSession the same evening. That's the smallest change. It's also the only change. Everything else is decoration.

**Q8: How do you find new apps — App Store search, TheSession recs, WhatsApp?**
**A8:** Never the App Store. Searching the App Store for trad gear is a fool's errand — you get a wall of guitar apps and karaoke yokes. TheSession is the main one. There's a tech-and-gear thread that's been running about six years, and if a post gets fifteen replies of "this is grand" I'll have a look. The session WhatsApp is the faster channel — twelve of us, somebody finds something useful on a Sunday and by Tuesday three of us have it on the phone. Enda Scahill mentions something in a video, that's an instant download for half the country. Joff Lowson the same. We're a small enough world that one good post in the right forum reaches Dublin to Boston to Melbourne overnight. It's a tiny scene but it's *connected*.

**Q9: Have you ever recommended an app to your session? How did that go?**
**A9:** Yeah, a few. Tunefox I shared in the WhatsApp years ago — most of them are still using it for tabs. Pro Metronome I pushed for the lad who can't keep time and he took it well enough. OAIM I've shared a hundred times. The flipside — there was an afinador app I tried once when I was on holiday and a session in Lisbon — I shared it before I'd properly tested it and it turned out to be a subscription racket. Two of the lads gave out to me for a fortnight. So now I sit on a recommendation for a week before I push it. My name's on the post forever, like. I'm not posting on TheSession about an app that ad-flashed me in front of the Whelan's lads.

**Q10: What's the biggest problem in a noisy pub for tuning?**
**A10:** The mic. Every single problem is the mic. You can have the best tuner ever written and if it's listening through a phone microphone in a bar with a football match on and twenty people talking, you might as well be trying to read a book in a thunderstorm. That's where a reference-tone app would actually win — *I* listen, not the phone. My ear is better than a phone mic in a pub by about a factor of ten. Give me the note out of the speaker, let me match it, leave the listening to me. That's the whole game in here.

**Q11: How do you tune the third string — the troublesome one?**
**A11:** The low G, you mean. It's the fat one and it's slow. The trick is you have to overshoot up slightly and let it settle down — string this thick has a memory, it'll creep flat on you over the first ten minutes if you tune it dead-on cold. Otherwise it's the same as the rest — fifth interval down off the D, listen for the beats, double-stop check against the open D. If you tune the G last and the bass of the pub is humming you can mistake harmonics for the fundamental and end up an octave wrong, which is the kind of mistake that gets you a slag in the WhatsApp the next day. So I tune G second-to-last and check it against itself in two octaves before I'm happy.

**Q12: Do you teach? Any students who struggle with tuning specifically?**
**A12:** I teach the music in the primary school and I take three private students on the tenor. Two of them are grand, one of them — God love her, she's eleven and keen as anything but the tuning is a battle every lesson. She can't yet hear when a string is sharp versus flat — she can hear "wrong" but not which direction. That's a developmental thing, she'll grow into it. What she *could* use is a reference-tone tool where she plays the note and the app plays the target and she trains her ear by matching, not by reading a needle. A needle teaches you to chase a dot. A tone teaches you to listen. I'd put a reference-tone app on her ma's phone tomorrow if it had GDAE.

**Q13: What's your relationship with "modern" music tech vs trad orthodoxy?**
**A13:** I'm not a purist about it. Tradition isn't a museum, it's a living thing — every generation brings whatever tools they have. The lads in the 1950s used radios and 78s; we use Voice Memos and TheSession.org. What I *am* purist about is taste. There's an unspoken thing in trad where if you're seen struggling with technology in front of the session, it undermines your credibility as a musician. The tool has to be invisible. Pull out your phone, get the note, phone away, play the tune. Anything that takes more than five seconds of fiddling is breaking the social contract. So it's not "no tech" — it's "tech that disappears."

**Q14: Would you pay €3 once to remove ads from an app? €5?**
**A14:** Three or five euro, once? I'd not even hesitate. I spend more than that on a single pint in Hughes' and at the end of the pint I've nothing to show for it but a wet glass. Five euro for a tool I'd use twice a week for years? That's the bargain of the century. What I will not pay is a subscription. I will not pay €1.99 a month for the rest of my natural life to tune a banjo. The day I sign up for a monthly direct debit to use a tuner is the day I go back to the fork permanently and lose another four of them.

**Q15: How does it feel when an ad interrupts you mid-tune?**
**A15:** Furious. There's no other word. You're concentrating, you've got the string ringing, your ear is locked onto the beat between your note and the reference, and a banner shoves up and the layout shifts and your finger hits a button you didn't mean to and now you're looking at a screen for a car insurance company. The concentration is gone. You have to start the whole listening process over. And if it happens twice in one session I'm done with the app for life — not in a "I'll try again next week" way, in a "deleted, told the WhatsApp, never spoken of again" way. The tuning surface is sacred. You can put ads on the splash screen, ads in the settings, ads in a *donate* tab. You cannot put ads where I'm listening.

**Q16: Is microphone permission a problem? Why or why not?**
**A16:** Not for me personally — I'll grant a mic permission if the app actually needs it. But here's the thing — a *reference-tone* tuner doesn't need it. So if your app asks me for the mic on first open and you're only playing tones, I'm going to think you're either incompetent or up to something. One of the things that'd actually be a *selling point* on TheSession is "no microphone permission required." That's the kind of detail the older lads notice and post about. Privacy is a quiet trad value — we don't want apps listening in the pub, we don't want apps phoning home about what tunes we played. Don't ask for what you don't need.

**Q17: What's "tuning by ear" vs "tuning to a needle"? Which side are you on?**
**A17:** Oh, by ear. Always by ear. Tuning to a needle teaches you to satisfy the screen. Tuning by ear teaches you to satisfy the *music*. Equal temperament on a needle is mathematically right but musically a wee bit cold — in trad we play with slides and ornaments and small bends, and a banjo that's exactly tempered against a fiddler who's playing slightly sharp on the leading tones is going to sound *worse* than two players who tuned by ear together at the start of the night. The needle is for guitar players in studios. The ear is for sessions. A reference tone, listened-to, is on the ear side of the line. A needle is on the wrong side. That's the whole philosophical case for what you're building, by the way — you just have to put GDAE on it.

**Q18: If Banjen added GDAE preset tomorrow, what would you do?**
**A18:** Download it that night. Test it Wednesday with the octave mandolin on the kitchen table, where I can be sure the tones are right against my fork. If it survives that, I bring it to Hughes' on Tuesday and use it for real. If it survives Tuesday — and "surviving Tuesday" means it doesn't ad-flash me, it doesn't crash, it gives me the note inside three seconds and shuts up — then Wednesday morning I'm posting on TheSession in the gear thread. Then I'm sharing in the session WhatsApp. Then by the weekend I'll have a half-dozen of the Galway lot using it and one or two of the Dublin crowd. Inside a month if it's good there's a couple of hundred Irish tenor players on it. That's not bragging, that's just how the network shape works. Small world, fast word.

**Q19: What's the worst thing a tuner app has done to you?**
**A19:** There was one — I won't name it, the lad who made it is probably grand — but it played a *full-screen video ad* the moment I opened it before the first session of a tour. Full sound on, in the pub, with my phone speaker pointed at the table. Every head turned. Cathal the box player said "Jesus Eileen what in the name of God." I'd to apologise, mute the phone, restart the app, and the same ad played again because I'd skipped it. Deleted it on the spot, in front of everyone, made a *production* of deleting it so they'd see I wasn't standing for that. That app is gone from my phone and gone from this session and gone from a fair few sessions because I told the story for about six months afterwards. Don't be that app.

**Q20: One sentence: pitch Banjen to your Tuesday-night session.**
**A20:** "Four-string banjo tuner, four big buttons, plays the actual notes so you can listen instead of the phone listening — GDAE, DGBD, no ads on the tuning screen, no signup, no microphone, costs less than a pint to make it ad-free forever — get it on yer phones lads, it works in Hughes'."
