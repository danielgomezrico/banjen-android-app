# Interview: Harold M., 67

*Retired accountant, Knoxville, TN. Returning to music after 50 years. DGBD 4-string banjo (Deering Goodtime).*

**Date:** 2026-05-20
**Interviewer:** UX Researcher (simulated)
**Format:** 20-question semi-structured

---

## Pull Quotes

> "I'm not afraid of sounding bad. I'm afraid of skipping a day. Then two days. Then... well. You see where that ends."

> "Linda thinks it's a hobby. I let her think that. It's not a hobby — it's a discipline. Like the morning paper used to be, like the office used to be. The banjo is what I have now."

> "I don't need the app to teach me. I need it to play me the note and then get out of my way. That's it. That's the whole thing."

---

## Q1: Walk me through the last time you tried to tune your banjo.

**A1:** Well... let me think. It was Tuesday morning, I believe. I sit down at seven o'clock — I'm pretty strict about that — and the first thing I do, before I even pick up the banjo, is I get the phone propped up on the music stand. I typed in "D G B D banjo reference tone" into the YouTube. And there's this fellow — I've seen him before — and he runs through about a two-minute introduction before he plays the first note. Talking about his channel and so on. Before that even started, though, there was a truck commercial. Ford or Chevy, I don't recall. Couldn't skip it for five seconds. Then Linda started the dishwasher in the kitchen — and our kitchen is not far from where I sit — and I couldn't hear the D. So I had to rewind. Rewinding triggered another ad. Hand to God. By the time I'd actually heard each of the four strings clean enough to tune by, it was 7:18. I had maybe twelve minutes of practice left before Linda wanted me to come look at something on her phone. So... that's the last time. Pretty typical, honestly.

## Q2: What goes through your head when you open a tuner app?

**A2:** Hesitation. That's the honest answer. I think... "okay, where's the button I tapped last time, and please don't have moved." I'm not proud of it but I do brace myself a little. The grandkids tell me I shouldn't worry — Marcus, that's my oldest grandson, he tells me "Grandpa, you can't really break it" — but I've broken things before. I once subscribed to something for ninety-nine cents a week and didn't notice for four months. So when I open one of these tuner apps, I'm sort of scanning for landmines. Where are the ads? Is something going to pop up? Is there a button that says "free trial" that I'd better not touch? It's like walking into a room where you're not sure where the furniture is. That's the feeling.

## Q3: What does "in tune" feel like to you — is it a sound, a feeling, a number?

**A3:** Oh, it's a feeling. Definitely a feeling. ... You know, I played trumpet in the high school marching band — this would've been '74, '75 — and I haven't really thought about it in fifty years, but the thing about a brass section, you *feel* when you're in tune with the fella next to you. There's like a... a hum. Or the absence of a hum. The two notes lock up and they stop fighting each other. That's what I'm chasing on the banjo. When the D from the app and the D off my string stop fighting — they just sort of merge — that's tuned. I don't trust numbers. I never trusted numbers, even as an accountant, not without checking them twice. But that feeling, when two notes settle into each other? That I trust.

## Q4: Tell me about a tuning attempt that failed badly. What did you do?

**A4:** Last Thursday. Before the jam circle at the community center. I had about twenty minutes at home before I needed to drive over, and I was nervous about it — they'd asked me to try playing along on "Wagon Wheel" — and I sat down and the YouTube reference tone fellow wasn't loading. Buffering wheel just spinning. I tried a different video and that one started with a long monologue about his trip to Nashville. I... I'll be honest with you, I got upset. Not crying upset, just — frustrated. I put the banjo down. I drove over with the banjo still out of tune and just asked Ray — that's my teacher, he runs the circle — if he'd tune it for me before we started. He did. Took him forty seconds. But I was embarrassed. A 67-year-old man asking another man to tune his instrument because the phone wouldn't behave. That stuck with me.

## Q5: Why an app instead of a clip-on tuner from the music store?

**A5:** Ray showed me one once — a Snark, the little clamp-on with the screen. The needle bounces around. I couldn't tell when it had stopped bouncing. Ray said "when the screen turns green, you're good" but it kept flickering between green and yellow and I didn't know if I was supposed to be turning the peg one way or the other. The other thing is — and this might sound silly — I already have the phone. I don't want one more thing to lose. Linda's been on me about clutter. The phone, I always know where it is. A little plastic thing on the headstock, I'd take it off, set it down on the side table, and that'd be the last time I'd ever see it.

## Q6: What apps have you tried? Why did you stop using them?

**A6:** Let me see. GuitarTuna — Marcus put that on for me. I opened it up and the banjo was there but when I tapped it, it said I needed something called Pro to access it. Pro is a subscription. I closed it and didn't open it again. There was a chromatic something — I don't remember the name. It assumed I knew what note I was looking for. It just sat there showing me letters and numbers and waiting. I didn't know what to do with it. And then YouTube, which isn't really an app I think of as a tuner, but that's mostly what I use. I stopped using the others because they assumed I knew more than I do. The ones that don't assume — those are the ones that try to charge me. Catch-22.

## Q7: What do you wish the app would just tell you?

**A7:** "You're close, Harold," or "You're way off, try the other direction." That's it. Like a patient teacher sitting next to me. Not a needle, not a number, not a green light. Just words. A real person, if they were tuning my banjo with me, would say "a little higher" or "you went too far, come back." That's the language I understand. The numbers and the needles, those are for people who already know what tuned looks like. I'm still learning what it sounds like.

## Q8: Where do you sit when you practice? What's around you?

**A8:** I sit in the den. It's the room off the kitchen — used to be where the kids did homework. There's a recliner I don't sit in (back hurts), a wooden chair I do sit in, a music stand Linda got me for my birthday three years ago, and a little side table for the phone and a cup of coffee. Window to my left, looks out at the yard. The dishwasher is through the wall to my right, which matters because when it runs I can't hear the banjo right. And the TV's in the next room — Linda watches the morning news. So I've got dishwasher, TV, sometimes the dog. It's not a quiet space. It's a lived-in space.

## Q9: What time of day do you tune?

**A9:** Seven o'clock in the morning. Sharp. That's been the time since I retired. I get up at 6:30, read for fifteen minutes — I read about... well, I read about a lot of things, brain health, music, whatever's on the AARP site — and then I'm in the chair with the banjo at seven. It's the cleanest part of my day. Linda's not really up and around yet, the phone hasn't started buzzing. If I miss seven, I'm done. I won't pick it up at noon or in the evening. The morning is the practice. Once that's gone, it's gone.

## Q10: Have you ever shown the app to anyone else?

**A10:** No. ... Honestly no. Because none of the apps have impressed me enough that I'd put one in front of Ray or anybody at the jam circle. If I showed Ray that I was tuning off a YouTube video with ads in it, he'd just laugh, or worse, he'd feel sorry for me. I don't want either one. The day I have an app I'd actually hand to Ray and say "look at this, this is how I tune now" — that'd be the day. Hasn't come yet.

## Q11: What would make you delete this app immediately?

**A11:** An ad popping up while I'm in the middle of tuning. Now I know that sounds like a small thing — and I know everybody's got to make money, I was an accountant, I understand a business model — but if I'm in the middle of turning a peg and the screen jumps and an ad covers up the button I was about to press, I'm done. That's the same nonsense as YouTube. If I'm paying with my attention, the deal is the attention pays during the *waiting*, not during the *doing*. You interrupt the doing, you've broken the deal. Delete. Same day.

## Q12: What would make you tell Linda "this thing's actually good"?

**A12:** *(pauses)* ... If I sat down at seven, opened the app, tapped a button, heard the D, tuned my string, did the same for the other three, and was playing by 7:02. Two minutes. No ads, no signup, no fellow talking about his Nashville trip. If three weeks went by and I'd tuned every morning without a single bad experience — that's when I'd say something to Linda. Probably wouldn't even be a recommendation. I'd just stop complaining. She'd notice. She always notices.

## Q13: If the app cost $3 once to remove ads, would you pay?

**A13:** Three dollars one time? Yes. That's a no-brainer. A set of strings is fifteen, twenty bucks. Three dollars to make the tuner stop fighting me — that's a steal. *Now* — if you said three dollars a *month*, I'd say no. Even three dollars a year I'd hesitate on, because the principle of the thing. I don't want a relationship with the app. I want to buy it once and have it work. Like a hammer. You don't subscribe to a hammer.

## Q14: What's the worst thing a tuner app could do during tuning?

**A14:** *(immediate)* Make a sound. A sound that isn't the note. An ad with audio, a notification, anything. Because when I'm tuning, my ear is doing all the work. I'm leaning in. I'm listening for that hum I told you about. If the phone makes a noise that isn't the reference tone, my ear loses the thread and I have to start over. And starting over costs me a minute, which on a fifteen-minute morning is real money. Visually too — if the screen jumps, if a button moves while I'm reaching for it — that's almost as bad. But sound is the worst. The worst.

## Q15: Has practicing the banjo changed anything else in your life?

**A15:** *(longer pause)* ... Yes. I get up easier. The morning has a shape to it now. When I was working, the shape was: shower, coffee, drive in, the office. The retirement gap — that first year, year and a half — I didn't have a shape. I'd sit with the paper for two hours and not really read it. The banjo gave me a seven-o'clock anchor. And it gave me the Thursday jam to look forward to, which means Wednesday I'm a little tuned up because I know tomorrow matters. So... yeah. It's not just the banjo. It's the rest of the week hanging off the banjo.

## Q16: What's the connection (if any) between tuning and your memory/brain?

**A16:** *(quieter)* ... Are you recording this? *(...)* All right. Look — Linda's mother went down with dementia. I watched it from about a year out from the diagnosis to the end. It's not something you forget. I read an article on the AARP website — and I've read others since — that said learning a musical instrument in your later years is one of the most protective things you can do. There was a study at the University of South Florida, I think. Better than crossword puzzles. So when I sit down at seven o'clock and I tune those four strings... I'm not just tuning a banjo. I'm — I don't want to be dramatic about it — I'm doing the work. And when the app gets in the way of the tuning, it's not just an annoying app. It's gotten in the way of *the work*. I haven't told Linda that part. She'd worry. Or she'd hover. I don't need either one. Don't put my name on this.

## Q17: How do you feel about apps that ask for microphone access?

**A17:** I don't like it. Marcus tells me it's fine, it's just to listen to the string. But I don't know what it's listening to or where the sound goes. I read in the paper — *the paper*, not online — about phones listening to people. So when an app asks for the microphone, I usually say no, and then the app doesn't work, and I delete it. If there's a tuner that doesn't need the microphone — that just plays the note for me to match — that's the one I want. That's actually what I've been doing on YouTube, when you think about it. The YouTube doesn't need my microphone. It just plays the note.

## Q18: Do you ever practice with the TV on, or other noise?

**A18:** Linda has the morning news on usually. I can mostly tune it out — I've been married forty-one years, I've had practice. But the dishwasher is the killer. And the dog. Roscoe, our beagle, he'll bark at the mail truck right around 7:15. So yeah, my tuning environment is not a recording studio. Which is another reason I don't trust the clip-on or the microphone tuners — they're trying to hear the banjo, but they're hearing Roscoe and the dishwasher and Lester Holt. If the app *makes* the sound instead of *listening* for it, that's better. The app's sound comes out of the phone speaker right next to my ear. I can hear that over Roscoe.

## Q19: If Banjen disappeared tomorrow, what would you do?

**A19:** *(thinks)* ... I'd go back to YouTube, I suppose. With the ads. Or I'd ask Ray if he'd record the four notes for me on his phone and just send them over so I could play them back. He'd do it. Ray's a good man. But I'd be back to the friction. And — this is the honest part — I'd probably tune less often. Skip a morning here, a morning there. And like I said earlier, that's the part that worries me. Not the banjo sounding bad. The skipping. That's what I'd lose.

## Q20: One sentence: what does Banjen need to fix?

**A20:** Don't put anything between me and the four notes — no ads, no signup, no menus, no surprises — just four big buttons that play the strings, every morning, the same way, forever.
