# Community Listener — Voice-of-the-Player Research

**Role:** community-listener
**Project:** Banjen (Android 4-string banjo tuner, DGBD reference tones)
**Date:** 2026-05-20
**Method:** Public forum sweeps (Banjo Hangout, Mandolin Cafe, The Session, Cifra Club, Reclame Aqui), Google Play / App Store reviews of competing tuner apps, Portuguese-language cavaquinho blogs and forums, and YouTube tutorial commentary. All quotes paraphrased or quoted from public threads; usernames anonymised by handle role (e.g., "older beginner," "forum elder," "cavaquinho student").

---

## Executive Summary

- **The tuning screen is a sacred contract.** Across every app reviewed (GuitarTuna, Cifra Club Afinador, Tuner ONE, Perfect Tuner, Pano Tuner), the single most common 1-star complaint is the same shape: "ads start the moment I press tune, I can't even hear my string." This is the v2 dealbreaker pattern, externally re-confirmed across 5 separate apps. The bar for Banjen is not "fewer ads" — it is "no ads on the tuning surface, full stop."
- **The 4-string DGBD ecosystem is much bigger than tenor-banjo-only.** DGBD is the standard tuning for Brazilian cavaquinho, an open-G shape used by tenor/plectrum banjo players, a viable retune for baritone uke (normally DGBE), and the lower four of a 5-string banjo. A single sound-bank serves all of these users — and the cavaquinho audience alone is enormous in PT/BR/Angola/Cape Verde.
- **"Tune by ear" is aspirational, not effortless.** Forum elders consistently tell beginners "get an electronic tuner first, your ear will come" — but the same elders praise reference-tone / drone-tone apps as the *bridge* between the two. Banjen's positioning (ear-training via reference tones) lands directly in that gap.
- **Older beginners have specific physical and cognitive friction points** that visual-needle tuners worsen: small tuner displays, fast-moving needles they can't read, friction pegs that overshoot, anxiety about being "tone deaf," and a self-imposed pressure to "catch up." A persistent, looping drone they can match without watching a needle removes most of these failure modes.
- **Brazilian / Portuguese-language demand is real and visibly underserved.** Reclame Aqui (Brazil's consumer-complaint clearinghouse) has standing complaint threads against Studio Sol's Cifra Club Afinador. Tuner ONE's App Store BR reviews explicitly describe "uma avalanche de anúncios" blocking the tune. Banjen's existing PT listing (Afinador Cavaquinho e Banjo) is already among the top organic results for "afinador cavaquinho" — the opportunity is to lean in, not test the waters.

### Theme 3: Beginner Anxiety ("am I tone deaf?")

23. *"Get an electronic tuner and don't worry about tuning by ear. Your ear will develop, but you'll still use the tuner."* — Banjo Hangout, "Tuning when tone deaf" thread. (Source: banjohangout.org/archive/295056) — Quoted by multiple respondents as the standard reassurance to anxious beginners.

24. *"Pitch recognition or perfect pitch just means learning to recognise the sound of notes so you can hear if a note is right or wrong. The banjo is a marvellous instrument for developing these skills because it is tuned to an open G chord."* — Banjo Hangout elder, same thread. (DGBD/open-G is *already* a training set; Banjen formalises that.)

25. *"When doing ear training for tuning by ear, focus on intervals: M3, P4th and P5th. If you can recognize these intervals, it's pretty easy to tune them off of your 3rd string."* — Banjo Hangout, "Tuning by ear methods?" (Source: banjohangout.org/archive/371298)

26. *"Some players tune the 3rd string by ear, then use relative tuning to tune the rest of the strings."* — same thread. (Workflow Banjen could explicitly support: "tune G first, then match the others.")

27. *"Tune up, not down — if you overshoot, drop below the note and tune up to pitch."* — Sweetwater banjo tuning guide. (Universal advice; Banjen could embed as a tooltip.)

35. *"Para quem já tem alguma prática, é possível afinar o cavaquinho usando apenas o ouvido, comparando o som de uma corda pressionada num determinado traste com a corda seguinte solta."* (For experienced players it's possible to tune by ear by comparing a fretted note against the next open string.) — Vianx. 36. *"Com a prática, o ouvido se torna mais sensível às nuances de afinação, tornando o processo mais intuitivo."* (With practice the ear becomes more sensitive to tuning nuances, making the process more intuitive.) — Cavaquinho.net.

37. *"In Brazil, the cavaquinho is nearly always tuned to an open G major chord (DGBD), with the exception of some players who find DGBE better for playing solos."* — Fret Expert. (English-language confirmation; relevant for diaspora Brazilians searching in English.)

38. *"Cavaquinhos are usually tuned DGBD, one octave higher than the baritone ukulele."* — Ukulele Underground forum. (Banjen tones at D3/G3/B3/D4 are roughly cavaquinho-octave — confirms the reference pitches are usable, possibly with an octave option as future work.)

## Unmet Needs Patterns

### Pattern A: "Don't break the tuning surface."
Every competitor breaks the tuning experience with ads, popups, subscription nags, or microphone permission loops. **Banjen wins by simply not doing this.** The v2 pain "ad layout shift during active tuning" is universal — confirmed across GuitarTuna (US), Cifra Club (BR), Tuner ONE (BR), Perfect Tuner (US/global), and Pano Tuner (global). Banjen's banner-only, never-overlay-during-playback rule is a real differentiator.

### Pattern B: "Give me a stable reference, not a dancing needle."
Beginners and older players explicitly struggle with fast-moving needle UIs (quote #10, #9). Drone-tone / reference-pitch apps (DroneTone, LikeTones, Banjen) are praised by elders as *the bridge* to ear training. The unmet need is **a reference that holds long enough for an older user to physically turn a friction peg without rushing.** Banjen's looping mp3 already does this — feature it explicitly in onboarding.

### Pattern C: "Help me trust myself."
Beginner anxiety quotes (#23–#27) show players asking "am I tone deaf?" Forum elders universally answer "no, the ear is a learned skill." Banjen's UX could embed micro-reassurance: "If it sounds close, it probably is. Trust your ear." This costs nothing and addresses a documented emotional friction point.

### Pattern D: "Don't make me read."
Older beginners (#28, #32) deal with vision and fine-motor friction. Big buttons, audio-first interaction, no needle-reading required — Banjen's current design accidentally nails this. **Lean in:** larger string-button labels, optional VoiceOver-friendly string names ("D, low D"), and a "hold to play" alternative for users who fumble the tap.

### Pattern E: "Speak Portuguese to me."
Brazilian-language searches dominate the cavaquinho ecosystem. Reviews in PT routinely contain words competing English apps don't index against ("afinador", "cavaquinho", "cavaco", "samba", "choro"). The Play Store listing should be PT-BR native, not just translated — and should include the words *cavaquinho*, *cavaco*, *afinação tradicional*, *samba*, *choro* in description copy.

### Pattern F: "Don't sell me a subscription for a tuner."
Subscription rage is the second-largest complaint category after "ads during tuning." Multiple users (#18, #21) explicitly compare the subscription cost to a $10 clip-on tuner and conclude "I should have bought hardware." Banjen's one-time-purchase / banner-ad model is the right shape; do not move to subscription.

## Vocabulary Glossary — Words Players Actually Use

| Term | Meaning / Player usage |
|---|---|
| **Drone tone / drone note** | A continuously sounding reference pitch, used both for tuning and for ear-training. Old-time banjo culture; also tuba / Indian classical pedagogy. |
| **Reference pitch / reference tone** | What teachers call the "known" note you're matching to. Often interchangeable with drone tone. |
| **Open G** | The DGBD/gDGBD chord shape the banjo (and cavaquinho) is tuned to — strumming open strings gives a G major chord. |
| **Relative tuning** | Tuning one string to a reference, then tuning the rest by fretting that string at the 5th fret and matching the next open string. Universal across stringed-instrument forums. |
| **Compensated bridge** | A bridge angled so that fretted notes match harmonics. Beginners discover this when "in tune open" ≠ "in tune fretted." |
| **5th-fret method** | The relative-tuning trick: open G ↔ 5th-fret D, etc. Cavaquinho players call this "comparando uma corda pressionada com a próxima solta." |
| **Sweet spot** | The bridge placement and tuning state where the whole instrument resonates. Old-time and clawhammer obsession. |
| **G string buzz / 5th-string buzz** | The classic banjo annoyance — drone string buzzing because it's not seated or is touching a fret. |
| **Tune up, not down** | Standard advice: always arrive at the target pitch from below, so the peg is under tension. |
| **Clawhammer / frailing** | Old-time playing style that benefits from drone-heavy tunings. The Harold M. demographic gravitates here. |
| **Afinador** (PT) | Tuner. Most common search term in BR Play Store. |
| **Afinação** (PT) | Tuning (the act / the state). "Afinação tradicional do cavaquinho" = DGBD. |
| **Cavaquinho / cavaco** (PT) | The Brazilian/Portuguese 4-string DGBD instrument. "Cavaco" is the colloquial Brazilian form, especially in samba contexts. |
| **De ouvido** (PT) | "By ear." Direct synonym to "by ear" — a learnable skill, not innate. |
| **Choro / samba** (PT) | Two of the genres cavaquinho players are tuning *for*. Mentioning these in Play Store copy signals "we get you." |
| **Chicago tuning** | DGBE on a tenor banjo or baritone uke. Adjacent to DGBD. |
| **GDAE** | Irish tenor banjo / mandolin / octave mandolin tuning. *Not* Banjen's audience today but a documented future expansion. |
| **CGBD plectrum tuning** | Plectrum-banjo standard. Forum threads compare it directly to DGBD ("Any advantage of CGBD over DGBD?"). |
