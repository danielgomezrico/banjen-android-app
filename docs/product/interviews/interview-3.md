# Interview: Betty L., 72
*Retired nurse, 40-year plectrum banjo veteran, Dixieland band*

---

**Q1: Walk me through the last time you tried to tune your banjo on your own. What happened step by step?**

A1: Well, it was last Tuesday before rehearsal. I got my banjo out of the case about an hour before I needed to leave — I always give myself extra time because you never know. I sat down in my chair by the window, that's where the light's best, and I thought, let me try that little clip-on thing one more time. So I got the Snark out of the drawer and clipped it on the headstock. I plucked the C string and that little screen started flickering — green, then red, then it said C#, then it jumped to B, and I'm sitting there thinking, well, am I sharp or flat? The letters were just changing too fast. My eyes aren't what they used to be, and with the glare from the window I could barely read it anyway. I fiddled with it for maybe five minutes, got more frustrated than I should've, and just put it back in the drawer. Drove to rehearsal early and asked Marcus — he plays piano — to give me my notes. Tuned up in about thirty seconds once I had the reference pitches. That's the thing that gets me. I *know* how to tune. I've been doing it since before some of these apps were a twinkle in somebody's eye.

---

**Q2: What's the most frustrating part of how you handle tuning today?**

A2: It's the dependency. That's the word for it. I spent thirty-five years as a nurse — I ran a floor, I made decisions that mattered, and now I have to show up twenty minutes early like a child who can't tie her shoes, just to ask somebody to play four notes for me. Marcus is a sweetheart about it, bless his heart, but last month he was late and I sat there for fifteen minutes with an out-of-tune banjo feeling useless. And some weeks I want to practice at home — just twenty minutes, that's all my hands give me these days — and I can't even do *that* properly because I don't have a reliable reference pitch at home. I've got good ears. I've always had good ears. But you need a starting point, and I don't have one I can count on when I'm by myself. It steals my independence, and at my age, you notice every piece of independence that gets taken away.

---

**Q3: What have you tried before? Why did it stop working?**

A3: Oh, honey, let me count the ways. I had a pitch pipe years ago — my husband Earl bought it for me, God rest him — and that worked fine until I lost it in a move. Then someone at rehearsal gave me that Snark clip-on. Like I said, the screen changes too fast, the letters don't mean anything useful to me in the moment. I know what C sounds like. I don't need a letter. I need to *hear* it. Then my daughter Colleen — she's very well-meaning — put a tuner app on my phone last year. I opened it one time and there were ads everywhere. I touched something, I don't even know what, and it started downloading something onto my phone. I panicked. I turned the phone off and called Colleen, and she had to come over and clean it up. I haven't opened that app since. It's probably still on there. I just don't trust it. And look, I know that's not rational — Colleen tells me I can't break the phone. But when you've had a bad experience, you avoid the thing that caused it. That's just human nature. I saw it every day as a nurse.

---

**Q4: If someone told you there was an app that could play the exact notes you need — what's the first thing that would make you trust it enough to try it? What would make you refuse?**

A4: The first thing? If Colleen or one of my bandmates installed it and showed me it was safe. I'm not going to the app store myself — I wouldn't even know how, honestly, and I don't want to know. But if Colleen put it on my phone and said "Mama, just press this button and it plays a C," I'd try it in a heartbeat. The second thing is it can't have ads that move around or pop up. That's what scares me — things that jump around on the screen, things I might accidentally touch. If I press something and it takes me away from what I was doing, I'm done. I'll put the phone down and I won't pick it back up. What would make me refuse? If it wanted my credit card, if it asked for permissions I don't understand, if it looked busy and complicated with a bunch of little icons and meters and things. I don't need a cockpit. I need four notes. And it has to loop — I can't hold the phone and turn a tuning peg at the same time. My hands don't cooperate like that anymore.

---

**Q5: What would you pay for a perfect solution? What are you comparing it to?**

A5: Well, I'll tell you what — if I could get something that just plays my four notes, reliably, every time, with no ads and no nonsense? I'd pay five dollars and be happy about it. Maybe even ten. I'm on a pension, so I watch my money, but this isn't a luxury. This is what keeps me playing in the band, and the band is... well, the band is important to me. It's where I see my people every week. My doctor would probably say it's good for me — she's always after me to stay active and social. So ten dollars for something that works every time and doesn't scare me? That's cheaper than one visit to the pharmacy. I'm comparing it to a pitch pipe, I suppose, which was maybe three or four dollars back when Earl bought it. But really I'm comparing it to free — because right now asking Marcus doesn't cost me anything except my pride.

---

**Q6: Who else in your Dixieland band deals with tuning? How do they handle it differently?**

A6: Well, Marcus doesn't tune — he's piano, so that's taken care of. Jimmy on trumpet has one of those electronic tuners he clips on, and he seems happy with it, but trumpet's different — he's checking one pitch, not four strings. Our clarinet player Darlene says she just uses an app on her phone, but Darlene is also sixty-two and she texts with her thumbs like a teenager, so that's apples and oranges. The trombone player, Robert, tunes to the piano just like I do. He shows up early too. We've joked about it — the "early bird tuning club." Now, here's the thing nobody talks about: the guitar player, Steve, he's maybe fifty, and he uses some app that plays the notes for him. Just plays them. He taps the string name, it plays the note, he tunes to it. I've watched him do it. That's exactly what I want, but when I asked him what app it was, he said it was for guitar and he didn't think it did banjo. And he said it had ads but he just ignores them. Well, I can't just ignore them — I'm liable to tap on one.

---

**Q7: Can you walk me through what happens when you open your phone to try to use an app?**

A7: Oh Lord. Okay. So I pick up the phone and I press the button on the side — Colleen showed me that. The screen comes on and I see my wallpaper, which is a picture of my grandkids. Then I swipe up, like Colleen showed me, and there are all these little squares. Now, half the time I can't remember which one is the app I want. They all look the same to me when they're that small. I squint at them, I might get my reading glasses if they're nearby. If I find the right one, I tap it, and then — this is where things go sideways — it opens and there's usually *stuff* everywhere. Banners at the top, buttons I don't recognize, sometimes things that move. My instinct is to not touch anything I don't understand, because last time I touched the wrong thing I ended up downloading something. So I freeze. I just look at it. And if it's not immediately obvious what to do — like, *immediately* obvious — I close it. I press the home button and I put the phone down. It's not that I'm dumb. I was charge nurse on a cardiac unit. I just don't have a mental map for how these things work, and every bad experience makes me less willing to try again.

---

**Q8: What would the ideal tuning tool look like for you — without worrying about whether it exists?**

A8: Four big buttons. That's it. Big enough that I can see them without my reading glasses and big enough that my arthritic fingers can tap them without hitting the wrong one. Each button says the string name — C, G, B, D — and when I press one, it plays that note. Loud and clear. And it keeps playing until I tell it to stop, because I need both hands on the banjo. No ads. No moving pictures. No settings I have to figure out. No little gear icons or hamburger menus or whatever Colleen calls them. Just the notes. If I'm being greedy, it would sound like a real instrument — a piano or something warm, not a robot beep. And the sound needs to come out clear enough that I can hear it even when I take my hearing aid out, because the aid distorts the high notes sometimes and I actually tune better without it. That's it. I don't need it to listen to my banjo and tell me if I'm sharp or flat. I've got forty years of ears for that. I just need the reference pitch.

---

**Q9: What would it take for you to recommend something to your daughter or your bandmates?**

A9: If it worked the first time Colleen put it on my phone, and it worked the second time I opened it by myself, and nothing scary happened either time — I'd tell the whole band about it. That's a low bar, I know, but it's the bar that nothing has cleared yet. I'd tell Robert first, because he's in the same boat as me — shows up early, tunes to the piano. And I'd tell Colleen to put it on her phone too, because she plays a little ukulele and she's always asking me to tune it for her, which is funny when you think about it. But here's the real thing: I would only recommend something if I felt like it respected me. Not respected me like a customer — respected me like a person who knows what she's doing musically but just needs a little help with the technology part. That Snark tuner, those apps with the bouncing needles — they treat you like you don't know what a note sounds like. I know what a note sounds like. Just play it for me.

---

**Q10: What am I not asking that I should be?**

A10: You should ask about the emotional part. Because nobody does. People think tuning is a technical problem — "oh, just get a tuner" — but for me it's wrapped up in a lot of other things. Earl used to play guitar, and when we'd practice together in the living room, he'd give me my starting notes. He'd just play the chord and I'd tune to it. It was part of our routine for twenty-some years. After he passed, I didn't have that anymore, and I'll be honest with you, the tuning problem is partly a grief problem. It's a reminder that my partner isn't there to do that small thing anymore. And the band — the band is everything right now. My doctor doesn't know this, but some weeks that Tuesday rehearsal is the only reason I get dressed up and leave the house. If I couldn't tune, if I couldn't play, if I had to drop out... I don't want to think about that. So when I say I need a simple way to get my reference pitches, I'm not being dramatic. I'm trying to hold on to the thing that keeps me going. And I think you should also ask about the hands. The arthritis is getting worse. I used to be able to tune in twenty seconds; now it takes me a minute because my fingers don't grip the pegs as well. So every second I spend fumbling with technology is a second I'm not spending on the part that already takes longer than it used to. Time matters more than it used to. That's not something a twenty-five-year-old app developer thinks about, but it's real.
