# Interview: Marcus T., 28
*Multi-instrumentalist, studio musician, Austin TX — qualified skeptic*

---

**Q1: Walk me through what happened the last time you tried to tune your tenor banjo for a recording session. What actually happened?**

A1: Okay so last Thursday I had a session at my buddy Rafa's home studio — we were tracking a Brazilian choro arrangement and I needed my cavaquinho and banjo both in tune to D-G-B-D. I grabbed my Boss TU-3 first but it's set up for guitar on my pedalboard, so I had to unplug, reroute, and then the banjo's acoustic anyway so the pickup signal was garbage. So I open GStrings on my Pixel, manually scroll to chromatic mode, pluck the D string, and I'm standing there in Rafa's living room trying to get a clean reading while his AC unit is humming. Took me like two full minutes just to get through all four strings because I had to keep re-selecting the target note. Meanwhile Rafa's already got his guitar tracked and he's looking at me like "bro, are we doing this?" The whole thing is just friction. Tuning should be invisible — it should take ten seconds max and then I'm playing. For an instrument with only four strings in a fixed tuning, the fact that I'm wrestling with a chromatic tuner is honestly embarrassing.

---

**Q2: You tried Banjen and deleted it — tell me exactly what happened.**

A2: Yeah, look, I found it on Reddit — someone in r/banjo mentioned it and I thought, "oh cool, something purpose-built for DGBD, that's exactly what I need." Downloaded it, opened it, saw the four buttons — D, G, B, D — and I was like, this is it, this is the thing. Clean, simple, reference tones, no chromatic nonsense. I tapped the D string, the tone started playing, I'm holding my banjo up to my phone, adjusting the tuning peg... and then a banner ad pops up at the bottom and the whole layout shifts. My finger accidentally taps the ad, it opens Chrome, and now I'm looking at some random website while my banjo string is still ringing out. I rage-quit. Closed everything, uninstalled it, and went back to GStrings. It's not that I'm anti-ads in principle — I get it, developers need to eat. But if you interrupt the actual tuning flow, the one thing your app does, you've lost me. I've been doing this for a while and I have zero patience for that. The irony is the core idea was perfect. Four buttons, reference tones, done. They just shot themselves in the foot with the monetization.

---

**Q3: You've got 4 tuner apps already. What would a new one have to do to earn a permanent spot on your phone?**

A3: It's not rocket science, honestly. First: it has to be faster than what I already have. My TU-3 is instant — clip on, pluck, done. Any app has to compete with that speed, which means zero configuration when I open it. Second: it needs to handle my actual workflow, which is multi-instrument. I go from guitar to banjo to cavaquinho to mandolin sometimes in the same session. I need presets — tap "banjo DGBD," tap "cavaquinho DGBD," tap "mandolin GDAE" — and it just switches. Third: no interruptions during tuning. No ads, no modals, no "rate this app" popups, nothing. And fourth — and this is the one nobody does — A=432Hz support. I record with some artists in Austin who are deep into the 432 thing, and when I'm tracking with them I need my reference tones pitched down. Right now I literally have to use a DAW to generate reference tones at 432. That's insane. Give me those four things and you've earned your spot. Miss any one of them and I'm sticking with what I have.

---

**Q4: What's the thing about tuner apps that nobody seems to get right? What's the obvious thing they keep missing?**

A4: Context switching. Every tuner app acts like you only play one instrument. You open it, you're in chromatic mode or you pick your instrument from some buried menu, you tune, you close it. Next instrument, you do the whole thing again. Nobody builds for the person who plays three instruments in one session. The other thing — and this is more subtle — is that reference tone apps and visual tuners are solving different problems but they're always jammed into the same app as an afterthought. I actually prefer reference tones for banjo because I'm training my ear and the banjo's intonation is weird enough that a visual tuner can be misleading. But for guitar I want visual. No app lets me say "for this instrument, give me reference tones; for this one, give me a chromatic display." They all pick one paradigm and force you into it. It's like nobody who builds these things actually plays multiple instruments in a studio setting.

---

**Q5: What would you pay for a perfect solution? What's your comparison point?**

A5: Look, I'm a barista who plays gigs. I'm not dropping $10 on a tuner app when GStrings is free. But here's the thing — I paid $100 for my Boss TU-3 without blinking because it solves the problem permanently with zero friction. So it's not that I won't spend money, it's that the value has to be obvious and immediate. For an app? I'd pay maybe $3-5 one-time for something that genuinely replaced my current workflow. Or — and this is what I'd actually prefer — free with no ads during the tuning flow. Put ads on a settings screen, put them on a "tip jar" page, I don't care. Just don't put them where I'm actively using the app. I'd even watch a 5-second ad on app launch if it meant the tuning screen was clean. My comparison point is literally the TU-3: zero friction, instant, always works. If an app can get within 80% of that experience, I'll keep it. Below that threshold, my phone is just a backup anyway.

---

**Q6: Who do you know who's solved this better than you have? What are they doing differently?**

A6: My friend Elena — she plays upright bass in a jazz combo here in Austin — she uses a Peterson StroboSoft app that cost her like $10. She swears by it. It's got sweetened tuning presets for different instruments, adjustable reference pitch, the works. But it's overkill for what I need and honestly the UI looks like it was designed in 2008. Then there's Diego in my Brazilian music group — he just uses a clip-on Snark tuner for his cavaquinho. Costs twelve bucks, lives on his headstock, works in any environment. No phone, no app, no ads, no configuration. He solved it by not using software at all. That's honestly the competition for any tuner app — a $12 clip-on. When your app is worse than a twelve-dollar piece of plastic, you have a product problem. The only advantage an app has over a clip-on is the reference tone thing — hearing the pitch and matching by ear. That's genuinely valuable for ear training. But if you bury that value under ads and bad UX, you've lost your one advantage.

---

**Q7: Show me your studio setup — how does tuning actually work for you in a real session?**

A7: Alright, so picture my spare bedroom — Scarlett 2i2 interface, Ableton on my laptop, a pair of KRK monitors, and then instruments everywhere. Guitar on a stand, banjo in the corner, cavaquinho hanging on the wall, mandolin in its case. When I'm tracking, I'll typically start with guitar because that's the backbone. I tune that with the TU-3 through my signal chain — takes five seconds. Then I switch to banjo, and this is where it gets stupid. I unplug the guitar, but the banjo doesn't have a pickup so the TU-3 is useless. I pull out my phone, open GStrings, set it to chromatic, pluck each string, squint at the display, try to block out the monitor bleed and the fridge humming in the next room. Two minutes minimum. Then if I need the cavaquinho for the same session, I do the whole phone routine again. The thing is, when I'm recording I'm in a flow state — I'm thinking about arrangements, about feel, about tone. Tuning should not be a context switch. It should be tap-tap-tap-done. A homescreen widget with four buttons that just plays reference tones? That would genuinely change my workflow. I wouldn't even have to unlock my phone and open an app.

---

**Q8: If you were building a banjo tuner app, what would it do that nothing does today?**

A8: First, I'd make it instrument-aware with presets. Banjo DGBD, cavaquinho DGBD, mandolin GDAE, Chicago tuning DGBE — all one tap away. Second, every preset gets a reference pitch slider: A=432 to A=446. Non-negotiable. Third, I'd have a homescreen widget — four buttons, one per string, zero app launch required. You tap the button, the tone plays, you tune, you tap again to stop. That's it. Fourth, and this is the key insight: the tuning screen is sacred. No ads, no banners, no nothing. You can monetize literally everywhere else. Put a tip jar, put a pro tier, put ads on the preset selection screen, I don't care. But the moment I'm actively listening to a reference tone and adjusting a tuning peg, that screen is mine. Fifth — and nobody does this — let me set different reference pitch per preset. Maybe my banjo is always at 440 but when I'm tracking with the 432 guys my cavaquinho needs to be at 432. Per-instrument pitch memory. That's the kind of thing that makes a musician go "oh, someone who actually plays built this."

---

**Q9: Under what conditions would you reinstall Banjen? What would have to be true?**

A9: Three things, and I mean all three. One: no ads during the tuning flow. Period. I don't care how they monetize — subscription, tip jar, ads on other screens, whatever. But the screen where I'm actively tuning cannot have ads. That's what made me delete it and that's the minimum bar to get me back. Two: a homescreen widget. Four buttons, one per string, tap to play the reference tone. This alone would make Banjen faster than any other option I have, including hardware. Three: adjustable reference pitch, at least A=432 to A=446. Without that I literally can't use it for half my sessions. If all three of those things were true, I'd reinstall it today and it would become my primary tuning tool for banjo and cavaquinho. I'd probably even recommend it on Reddit. But look — I've been burned before. If I reinstall and an ad shifts the layout while I'm tuning, it's gone forever. They get one more chance with me. One.

---

**Q10: What am I not asking that I should be?**

A10: You're not asking about the cavaquinho thing, and you should be. DGBD tuning isn't just banjo — it's also standard cavaquinho tuning. There's a whole Brazilian music community, especially here in Austin and in cities like Newark, Boston, LA, that plays cavaquinho. These are people who would use a DGBD reference tone app every single week at rodas and rehearsals. They're completely underserved — there's basically no cavaquinho-specific tuner app. If Banjen positioned itself as "the DGBD tuner" instead of just "the banjo tuner," you'd pick up a whole user segment that nobody's even thinking about. Diego in my group alone would install it tomorrow. And you're not asking about latency. When I tap a button and a reference tone starts playing, how fast does it start? If there's even a half-second delay, it feels broken. My TU-3 is instant. GStrings is instant. That responsiveness isn't a feature, it's table stakes. Also — you should ask about what happens when I'm wearing headphones during recording. If I'm tracking and I need to check my tuning without blasting a reference tone through the monitors, I need the app to route audio to my headphones or my interface. That's a real use case that nobody thinks about because nobody who builds tuner apps actually records music.
