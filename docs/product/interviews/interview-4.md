# Interview: Rafael S., 38
*São Paulo-born cavaquinho player, choro roda musician, NYC Brazilian music scene*

---

**Q1: Walk me through the last time you had trouble tuning your cavaquinho before a gig. What happened?**

A1: Ah, cara, this was two Saturdays ago at the choro roda at the restaurant in Astoria. We play every Saturday night — it's me, the violão de sete cordas player, pandeiro, flauta, the whole roda. I got there a little early, maybe 7:15, and the restaurant was already packed. People talking, dishes clinking, the kitchen going full blast. I pulled out my cavaquinho and opened that Cavaquinho Tuner app — the one I always use — and the thing just froze on the splash screen. I closed it, opened it again, same thing. So now I'm sitting there with an out-of-tune instrument and the roda starts in fifteen minutes. I tried using a clip-on tuner but with all that ambient noise, it was picking up everything — the TV above the bar, conversations, someone's kid crying. The needle was jumping everywhere. I ended up having to wait for Marcos, our violão player, to arrive so I could tune off his strings. It works, but it's embarrassing, you know? Like I'm a beginner who can't tune his own instrument. I've been playing cavaquinho since I was fourteen in São Paulo. I shouldn't need someone else to get my tuning right.

---

**Q2: What's the most frustrating part of how you handle tuning today?**

A2: The most frustrating thing is the noise, mano. Every gig I play is in a loud environment — restaurants, street festivals, parties. A mic-based tuner is completely useless to me. I need to HEAR the note, put it in my ear, and match it. That's how I learned in São Paulo, that's how every cavaquinho player I know does it. But finding an app that just plays me a clean D-G-B-D reference tone? It's like the app developers don't know my instrument exists. I have to go through this whole process every time — open the app, hope it works, deal with whatever bugs it has today. And the other frustration is the setup time. When I use a generic tuner, I have to manually dial in each note. D3, G3, B3, D4. Every single time. The app doesn't remember that I'm a cavaquinho player. It treats me like I'm configuring a synthesizer, not tuning an instrument I play every week.

---

**Q3: What apps or tools have you tried? Why did they stop working for you?**

A3: OK so the main one is the Cavaquinho Tuner app — I found it by searching "afinador de cavaquinho" on the Play Store. Because that's how I search, in Portuguese. When it works, it's actually pretty good — it knows my tuning, it plays the reference tones. But the developer, I think it's one person in Brazil, they stopped updating it. It crashes, it freezes, sometimes the audio just doesn't play. I reported bugs but nothing happens. Then I tried GuitarTuna because everyone recommends it. But cavaquinho isn't even listed as an instrument. Banjo is there but it's behind a paywall, and anyway their banjo is five strings, not four. I tried a generic chromatic tuner but again, I have to manually set everything and in a loud restaurant it can't hear my instrument over the noise. I bought a clip-on Snark tuner — that actually works OK in quiet rooms, like at home practicing. But at a gig? Forget it. The vibrations from the table, people bumping into things, it picks up everything. So I'm back to either the buggy cavaquinho app or asking Marcos for his strings. It's 2026, cara. How is this still a problem?

---

**Q4: If you found a new app that claimed to work for cavaquinho — what would make you trust it? What would make you immediately stop using it?**

A4: Trust? First thing — if I see "cavaquinho" anywhere in the listing, I'm already interested. Even just seeing DGBD tuning mentioned. That tells me someone thought about my instrument. Second, if the reference tones sound good. Clean, clear, not some cheap synthesized beep. I want to hear something that sounds like a real string being plucked, or at least a warm tone I can match to. Third, if it just works — I open it, I tap a button, I hear the note. No account creation, no tutorials, no "rate this app" popup. What would make me stop using it immediately? Ads that play audio. If I'm trying to tune my instrument and an ad starts blasting sound through my phone, I'm done. Vai lá, that's the worst thing you can do in a tuner app. Also if it's slow to load — if I'm between sets at the restaurant and I have maybe two minutes, I can't wait thirty seconds for the app to initialize. And if the tones are wrong — if D doesn't sound like D, I'll know instantly. My ear is trained. Bad tones and I delete the app and never come back.

---

**Q5: What would you pay for a perfect solution? What are you comparing it to?**

A5: Honestly? For something that works perfectly every time — plays me clean DGBD tones, loads fast, no bugs — I'd pay maybe five, six dollars once. That's like one açaí bowl, you know? It's nothing. But here's the thing: I compare it to free. The Cavaquinho Tuner app is free. My clip-on was twelve dollars and it's a physical object. YouTube is free — I can literally search "cavaquinho tuning DGBD" and find a video that plays the notes. The problem with YouTube is ads before the video, buffering, and I need internet. So a dedicated app is better, but the bar for "worth paying for" is high because free alternatives exist even if they're bad. What I'd really pay for is no ads. If an app works great but has banner ads, fine, I'll live with it. But if there's an option to pay three dollars to remove them? I'd do it today. I spend more than that on cafézinho every morning. The comparison isn't other tuner apps — it's the cost of minor daily annoyances I already tolerate.

---

**Q6: Who else in your choro group deals with tuning problems? How do they handle it differently?**

A6: Marcos, our violão de sete cordas player, he uses a clip-on tuner and it works OK for him because the seven-string guitar has more vibration, more sustain, so the clip-on picks it up better even in noise. The flauta player, she tunes from us — she adjusts to whatever pitch we're at, which is how it works in choro traditionally. The pandeiro player, well, he doesn't tune, he just hits things, haha. But in my WhatsApp group — I'm in a group with like forty-something Brazilian musicians in the NYC area, not just choro, also samba, pagode, forró — tuning comes up a lot. The cavaquinho players all have the same problem. Some of them use pitch pipe apps. Some of them literally have a tuning fork. In 2026! A metal tuning fork! One guy, Thiago, he keeps a short voice memo on his phone of himself playing each open string in tune, and he plays it back to tune. That's creative but also kind of sad that it's the best solution he found. The guitarists and violão players are fine — there are a million apps for them. It's specifically the cavaquinho and bandolim players who struggle.

---

**Q7: Show me your phone — what music apps do you use? How do you search for them?**

A7: OK so on my phone — it's a Samsung Galaxy A54, not the newest but it works — I have WhatsApp obviously, that's the first thing. For music I have YouTube for watching choro tutorials and performances. I have Instagram where I promote our gigs and follow other Brazilian musicians. I have the Cavaquinho Tuner app, which you know about. I have a metronome app, just a simple one. And Spotify for listening. That's basically it for music. When I search for apps, I search in Portuguese first, always. "Afinador de cavaquinho," "metrônomo," things like that. If I don't find what I need, then maybe I'll try English, but my first instinct is Portuguese. I also find apps through WhatsApp — if someone in the group shares a link, I'll click it. That's actually how I found the Cavaquinho Tuner app, someone shared it in the group years ago. Word of mouth through WhatsApp is how almost everything spreads in the Brazilian community here. I would never, ever search for "banjo tuner." That's an American thing. Even though the tuning is the same, the cultural connection isn't there. If you told me a banjo app would work for my cavaquinho, I might try it, but I would never discover it on my own.

---

**Q8: What's a feature you'd love that you've never seen anyone build for a tuner app?**

A8: Oh, this is a good question. You know what I really want? I want an app that plays all four strings in sequence automatically — D, then G, then B, then D — with maybe a five-second gap between each one. Like a little tuning routine. Because right now I have to tap each string one at a time, tune it, then tap the next one. If the app could just walk me through all four strings automatically, I could focus entirely on tuning instead of also operating my phone. Especially useful when my hands are on the cavaquinho and I don't want to keep reaching for the phone. The other thing — and nobody has done this — is a "noisy environment" mode that plays the reference tone LOUD through headphones. Like, really loud and clear, cutting through restaurant noise. One earbud in my left ear playing the reference tone, my right ear hearing my instrument. That would be perfect for gig situations. Also, honestly? Portuguese language support. Just basic Portuguese. It would make me feel like the app was made for someone like me, not just translated as an afterthought.

---

**Q9: If you found something that worked perfectly for your cavaquinho, what would you do next? How would you tell people?**

A9: Mano, the first thing I would do — literally the first thing — is share it in the WhatsApp group. I'd send a message like "Galera, achei um afinador que funciona! Olha isso" with a screenshot and the Play Store link. That group has forty-something musicians and probably fifteen of them play cavaquinho or bandolim. If even half of them try it and it works, they'll share it in THEIR WhatsApp groups. That's how things spread in the Brazilian community — it's not app store reviews, it's not Twitter, it's WhatsApp. One share in the right group and suddenly two hundred people know about it. I'd also tell people at the choro roda in person. Show them on my phone during a break. "Look, I just tap here and it plays D-G-B-D, no bugs, fast." Musicians are practical — if they see something working in front of them, they want it. I might post about it on Instagram too, like a story showing me tuning with the app before a gig. But WhatsApp is the main channel. That's how information moves in our world.

---

**Q10: What am I not asking that I should be?**

A10: You should be asking about the cavaquinho community specifically — not just me, but the size of this market. There are MILLIONS of cavaquinho players in Brazil. Millions. It's one of the most popular instruments in the country. Samba, pagode, choro — cavaquinho is in all of them. And the Brazilian diaspora is everywhere — New York, Boston, Miami, Newark, Japan, Portugal, all over Europe. Every city with a Brazilian community has cavaquinho players, and all of us have the same tuning problem. We're invisible to American app developers because we search in Portuguese, we communicate through WhatsApp, and our instrument shares a tuning with the banjo but has zero cultural overlap. You should also ask about bandolim — the Brazilian mandolin. Different tuning, but the same problem: no good dedicated app. And you should ask about the emotional side. When I'm in tune and the roda is flowing — everyone locked in, the melodies interweaving — that's the best feeling in music. Being out of tune ruins that for everyone. It's not just a technical problem. It's about being able to fully participate in a musical tradition that means everything to me. The tuning app isn't just a tool. It's the gateway to that experience.
