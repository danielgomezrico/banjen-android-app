# Banjen — Final User Personas
*February 23, 2026 | Persona Refiner — UX Design Thinking Investigation*

---

## Executive Summary

- **Who this is for**: Adults who tune 4-string instruments by ear — primarily DGBD banjo players, but also GDAE Irish tenor banjo players, CGBD plectrum players, and DGBD cavaquinho players. The common thread: they need reference tones, not visual chromatic displays, and they are failed by every existing tool.
- **Who this is NOT for**: 5-string bluegrass banjo players (the largest banjo segment, but incompatible with the current product). Jake R. from v1 has been replaced by Rafael S. — the cavaquinho crossover is a higher-leverage opportunity that requires zero code changes.
- **Biggest findings**: (1) Harold's true motivation is cognitive health, not hobby — tuning friction threatens his brain-health discipline. (2) Betty's tuning problem is also a grief problem — her late husband used to give her starting notes. (3) The "15-second fumble gap" (Siobhan's term) is the universal product problem across all personas. (4) Cavaquinho crossover confirmed independently by both Rafael and Marcus — millions of underserved players share the exact DGBD tuning.
- **Highest-leverage opportunities**: (a) Portuguese ASO and app store localization — zero code changes, unlocks millions of cavaquinho players. (b) Ad-free tuning screen — the single change that would retain Marcus and protect Harold and Betty. (c) GDAE preset — unlocks Siobhan's viral distribution channel across Irish session networks. (d) Session mode (auto-advance through strings) — requested independently by both Siobhan and Rafael.
- **What changed from v1**: Every persona gained emotional depth that reframes the product from "tuner" to "gateway." Harold isn't learning a hobby; he's fighting cognitive decline. Betty isn't tuning a banjo; she's holding onto the social lifeline that replaced her husband. Siobhan isn't a user; she's a distribution channel. Rafael replaces Jake as the high-leverage adjacent market. Marcus is the canary whose specific objections explain non-conversion at scale.

## Persona 1: Harold M., 67
*Retired accountant returning to music after 50 years, using banjo practice as a daily cognitive health routine*

**Location:** Suburban Knoxville, Tennessee
**Income:** $55,000/year (pension + Social Security)
**Tech savviness:** 2/5 — Uses smartphone for calls, texts, weather, YouTube. Grandkids set up new apps. Comfortable with repetitive workflows once learned; anxious about anything unfamiliar.
**Decision authority:** Full. Spends his own money, no one to convince. But cautious — compares every app purchase to a physical product ("Is this worth more than a set of strings?").

### Goals
- **Primary:** Learn to play banjo well enough to join the Thursday evening bluegrass jam circle at the community center
- **Secondary:** Maintain a structured daily routine that replaces the discipline of his 35-year accounting career
- **Hidden:** He read an AARP article about music and brain health after watching Linda's mother decline from dementia. He hasn't told Linda this is part of why he practices every morning. The neuroprotective angle is quietly his strongest motivator — stronger than the jam circle, stronger than the hobby. He fears cognitive decline more than he fears sounding bad.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** Cannot tell if his banjo is in tune — or so he thinks. He played trumpet in his high school marching band 50 years ago and may have dormant aural skills he's forgotten about. He sometimes wonders if he could "get it back" with a reliable reference tone to practice against.
- **Daily friction:** Spends more time fighting technology than playing. His YouTube workaround involves 30-second unskippable ads, talking before reference tones start, kitchen noise drowning out the sound, and rewinding that triggers more ads. He estimated 15 minutes fighting his phone for 45 seconds of actual reference tones.
- **What keeps him up at night:** Not just fear of wasting money on a dusty instrument — fear that skipping practice means cognitive decline wins. "When I sit down with that banjo at seven AM, part of what I'm doing is exercising my brain. If I get frustrated enough to skip a day, then two days, then a week — that scares me more than sounding bad at the jam."
- **Previous failed attempts:** GuitarTuna (paywall before trial), Snark clip-on ("the needle jumps around like a cat on a hot tin roof"), generic chromatic tuner (assumed he already knew target notes), YouTube reference tones (ad-laden, noisy, couldn't rewind without more ads).

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Asks his banjo teacher Ray to tune at weekly $40 lessons, consuming 5-10 minutes of paid time. Between lessons, he "babies the instrument" — plays carefully to avoid knocking it out of tune. Also searches YouTube for DGBD reference tones, which is literally what Banjen replaces.
- **Tools they use:** Samsung Galaxy phone (2 years old), YouTube for Jim Pankey's beginner lessons, Samsung voice recorder (records himself playing back), spiral notebook for tab, physical metronome, GuitarTuna (unopened since paywall), Google
- **Where they spend time online:** YouTube (85% of adults 50-64 use it — Pew 2025), Facebook (74% of adults 50-64), Banjo Hangout (reads but rarely posts), local Facebook group for the jam circle
- **Decision triggers:** If Ray (his teacher) specifically recommends it by name. If a friend at the jam says "here, try this." Teacher recommendations are the #1 acquisition channel for this demographic.

### A Day in Their Life
Harold wakes at 6:30, has coffee, and reads about how learning a musical instrument protects against dementia — he won't tell Linda, but this is part of why he sits down with his Deering Goodtime at 7:00 sharp. He opens YouTube, types "D G B D banjo reference tone," waits through a 30-second truck dealership ad, then listens to a fellow talk for two minutes before playing a note. Linda starts the dishwasher. He can't tell if his string matches over the noise. He rewinds, gets another ad. Fifteen minutes of fighting his phone yields forty-five seconds of reference tones. He plays his exercises knowing it sounds "a little off." The frustration dims his quiet pride in showing up every morning — and dims something deeper he won't name.

### Interview Insights
1. **The tuning problem costs $25-30/month in wasted lesson time.** Harold calculated it: 5-10 minutes of his $40/hour lesson goes to teacher tuning, which is $6-7 per lesson, four lessons a month. The app doesn't need to be perfect — it just needs to beat the pianist-dependency workaround.
2. **He has dormant aural skills he's forgotten.** Played trumpet in his high school marching band under Mr. Halverson, who'd play an A on the piano and the band would tune to it. Harold wonders if a reliable reference tone could reactivate that ear. This reframes the product from "tuner" to "ear training catalyst."
3. **His dream feature makes him independent of the app.** "Something that teaches me to hear the difference... so that eventually I don't need the app at all." He doesn't want a better tuner. He wants a teacher that makes him self-sufficient. This is the opposite of a sticky-retention model.
4. **Recommendation happens by demonstration, not description.** "If I could pull out my phone at the jam circle, tap one button, hear the tone, tune my string, and have Don or Kathy say 'what's that app?' That's how recommendations work in our group."
5. **Offline is critical.** The community center jam has terrible WiFi. If the app needs internet to play a reference tone, he can't use it where he needs it most.

### In Their Own Words
> "I spent fifteen minutes fighting with my phone and maybe forty-five seconds actually listening to reference tones. It's like trying to balance a ledger with half the numbers missing."
*Source: Harold M. interview, Q1*

> "The goal isn't to be dependent on a phone forever — the goal is to get good enough that I can hear it myself. I had that sense once, back in the trumpet days."
*Source: Harold M. interview, Q8*

> "When something as basic as tuning creates a barrier, it's not just annoying. It's threatening the whole routine... For me, it's staying sharp. Staying myself. The banjo is just the vehicle."
*Source: Harold M. interview, Q10*

### Product Fit
- **Why he'd choose this:** Plays the actual tone. He can hear it and match it — no screen to interpret, no confusing needle, no letter names he doesn't know. Four buttons, four strings, picture of a banjo. Trust comes from immediacy: "No sign-up screen, no tutorial I have to swipe through, no asking for my email. Just — here are your four strings, press one, hear the tone." His $40/lesson tuning tax makes even a $5 app a clear financial win.
- **Why he'd walk away:** "A big flashing ad before I've even done anything. Or one of those ads that takes over the whole screen and you can't find the tiny X to close it." A single bad ad experience causes permanent deletion. Account creation is a dealbreaker ("I'm tuning a banjo, not applying for a mortgage"). Music terminology he doesn't understand. Any perception of subscription pricing.
- **Willingness to pay:** $3-5 one-time purchase. Compares to the $18 Snark sitting in a drawer, the $5 coffee-and-donut at the Saturday spot, and the $25-30/month in wasted lesson time. Subscriptions are a non-starter: "I'm retired on a fixed income. I'm not signing up for another monthly bill."

**Surprise:** His dream feature is the *opposite* of retention — he wants the app to make him independent of it. "Play the reference tone, then let me pluck my string, and tell me 'you're close' or 'way off, tighten that peg.' Almost like a patient teacher sitting next to me." This reframes product strategy from engagement metrics to empowerment outcomes.

## Persona 2: Siobhan K., 34
*Irish traditional musician who plays tenor banjo in weekly pub sessions and represents a dense, interconnected ecosystem of GDAE players*

**Location:** Dublin, Ireland (uses English-language apps; sessions in pubs with poor cell reception)
**Income:** EUR 42,000/year (primary school teacher)
**Tech savviness:** 4/5 — Comfortable with technology, uses multiple music apps, participates in the IrishBanjoLessons.com Discord (~400 active members). But in sessions, wants zero tech friction.
**Decision authority:** Full. Makes small purchases without hesitation. More importantly, she's a **recommendation multiplier** — one endorsement from her reaches 20+ session players, and a post on The Session reaches thousands of lurkers worldwide.

### Goals
- **Primary:** Stay perfectly in tune during fast-paced pub sessions where stopping to re-tune is socially unacceptable — she has 20-30 seconds between tune sets
- **Secondary:** Have a single reference-tone tool that works for her tenor banjo AND her octave mandolin (both GDAE), replacing the tuning forks she keeps losing
- **Hidden:** She wants to be the person who discovers and recommends the perfect tool to her session network. Being a trusted voice on gear and tools is part of her musical identity. She frames herself as a distribution channel and she *wants* to recommend — but only something she'd trust publicly.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** The "15-second fumble gap" — the time between realizing she's out of tune and hearing a reference tone. That gap IS the product problem. Every second of fumbling (finding a fork, navigating an app menu, scrolling to the right tuning) is a second stolen from the 25-second window before the next set starts.
- **Daily friction:** Her tuning forks — acoustically perfect and silent — keep physically disappearing. She's on her fourth A fork this year. "They fall out of pockets, roll under tables, end up in the hoover at school." She's taped one inside her case; the tape residue drove her mad. The app must be *less losable* than a metal fork.
- **What keeps her up at night:** "If you're fumbling with an app while everyone is starting the next set, you just don't play that set. And if that happens twice, people notice." In Irish sessions, being seen struggling with technology undermines musician credibility. The tool must be invisible — phone face-down, one earbud, 20 seconds total, zero screen interaction.
- **Previous failed attempts:** Cleartune (EUR 4, "lovely app in a quiet room, completely useless in Whelan's on a Friday"), clip-on tuners ("vibrations from the table travel through the clip and give you phantom readings, plus I looked ridiculous"), PitchLab ("the interface is like a cockpit instrument panel"), guitar tuner apps ("ignoring the strings I don't need — a humiliating workaround for a skilled musician").

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Tuning forks, when she can find them. She tunes one string (A or D) from the fork and derives the rest by fifths — a standard skill among trad players. She doesn't need all four strings played; she needs ONE reliable reference pitch, fast. No dedicated tuner app currently on her phone.
- **Tools they use:** iPhone 14, Tunefox, Safari bookmark to thesession.org, OAIM (Online Academy of Irish Music), WhatsApp for session scheduling, Voice Memos (records sessions to learn tunes by ear), Spotify, Pro Metronome
- **Where they spend time online:** The Session (thesession.org — primary hub, thousands of lurkers per thread), Irish music Facebook groups, Comhaltas forums, OAIM, IrishBanjoLessons Discord (~400 active GDAE players)
- **Decision triggers:** If a respected session player recommends it. If she sees it on The Session. "A recommendation there from someone with a decent post history carries enormous weight. One thread called 'Finally a tuner that works for tenor banjo' and you'd have downloads from Dublin to Boston to Melbourne overnight." If Enda Scahill or Lisa Canny endorsed it, she'd install immediately.

### A Day in Their Life
Siobhan finishes school at 3pm, grabs her banjo case and her octave mandolin bag, and heads to Hughes' pub for the Tuesday session. The radiator is blasting next to her seat. She arrives 15 minutes early but the pub is already loud with conversation and a football match. She opens her case, digs for the A tuning fork — it's slipped through the gap in the lining again. She strikes it on her knee, gets the A string sorted, then tunes D, G, E by fifths. The pub is too loud and she can't tell if the G is right. Between the first and second tune in the set, she adjusts — but by then she's already sat out the first tune "like a lemon." She spends the rest of the night self-conscious about the G string.

### Interview Insights
1. **The fumble gap is 15 seconds, not the tuning itself.** "The frustration isn't really about the *tuning* itself. It's about the 15 seconds of fumbling and searching before you can even start tuning." The product problem is latency-to-first-note, not tuning accuracy.
2. **GDAE is a tuning SYSTEM, not a single instrument.** "Everyone focuses on 'banjo' but a huge number of Irish session players double on octave mandolin — same GDAE tuning, different instrument." One tuning preset serves tenor banjo + octave mandolin, doubling the addressable users.
3. **Social invisibility is a design requirement.** "Any tool that draws attention to itself — a bright screen, a loud notification, anything that makes someone look at you and think 'she's using an app' — that's actually worse than being slightly out of tune." The app must be usable phone face-down, one earbud, invisible to the table.
4. **She independently designed "session mode."** Auto-advance through strings with a timer: "plays G for five seconds, then moves to D, then A, then E, with maybe a little vibration or flash between each one so you know it's moving on even if you can't see the screen." Five seconds per string, twenty seconds total.
5. **Her network amplification is massive and specific.** One Tuesday night recommendation reaches the table. One post on The Session reaches Dublin-to-Boston-to-Melbourne overnight. The IrishBanjoLessons Discord has 400 active GDAE players. She is not a user — she is a distribution channel.

### In Their Own Words
> "The frustration isn't really about the tuning itself. It's about the 15 seconds of fumbling and searching before you can even start tuning. In a session, those 15 seconds are everything."
*Source: Siobhan K. interview, Q2*

> "There's an unspoken thing where if you're seen struggling with technology, it undermines your credibility as a musician. The tool needs to be invisible. Phone face-down, one earbud, done in twenty seconds, nobody notices."
*Source: Siobhan K. interview, Q10*

> "If I used it at a session and it actually solved the problem — I tuned quickly, I didn't miss a set, it was painless — I'd tell people that night. One thread called 'Finally a tuner that works for tenor banjo' and you'd have downloads from Dublin to Boston to Melbourne overnight."
*Source: Siobhan K. interview, Q9*

### Product Fit
- **Why she'd choose this:** Reference-tone approach works in noisy pubs. Offline functionality is non-negotiable. If GDAE were a first-class tuning preset — "right there, not buried in a settings menu, not 'custom tuning'" — she'd switch instantly and become an evangelist. Trust signal: seeing GDAE on the first screen tells her "the developer actually knows my instrument exists."
- **Why she'd walk away:** No GDAE = no install. Period. "If it opens and the first thing I see is a 5-string banjo graphic, that tells me this app isn't for me." Full-screen ad on launch is instant deletion. Account creation or sign-in requirement. Any onboarding flow that adds latency to the fumble gap. If it requires internet — "not every pub in rural Ireland has reliable mobile data."
- **Willingness to pay:** EUR 4-5 without hesitation. "I spend more than that on a pint." Compares to EUR 6 tuning forks she replaces four times a year (EUR 24/year on forks she keeps losing). Would "happily pay to remove ads" — prefers paying upfront over living with ad anxiety during sessions.

**Surprise:** The tuning fork — her current tool — is acoustically perfect and she knows it. The app isn't competing with bad tuning forks; it's competing with *good* tuning forks that are physically losable. The advantage isn't sound quality — it's that a phone doesn't roll under the table.

## Persona 3: Betty L., 72
*Retired nurse and 40-year plectrum banjo veteran whose declining eyesight and arthritis make technology a barrier to the music that keeps her socially alive*

**Location:** New Orleans, Louisiana
**Income:** $40,000/year (pension)
**Tech savviness:** 1/5 — Has an Android phone her daughter Colleen set up. Calls and texts only. Clinically documented technology anxiety: fear of accidental purchases, embarrassment at not understanding interfaces, avoidance spiral that prevents skill acquisition.
**Decision authority:** Full on small purchases, but relies on Colleen or bandmates to install and demonstrate apps. Won't download apps she doesn't trust. Colleen is the real gatekeeper.

### Goals
- **Primary:** Keep her plectrum banjo in standard CGBD tuning for weekly Dixieland band rehearsals — the band is her primary social lifeline since Earl passed
- **Secondary:** Maintain her independence as a musician; stop arriving 20 minutes early to ask Marcus the pianist for reference notes
- **Hidden:** Her doctor suggested "staying active with music" after a mild depression screening. The band isn't just a hobby; it's her healthcare. Some weeks, Tuesday rehearsal is the only reason she gets dressed up and leaves the house.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** She has 40 years of playing experience and excellent relative pitch — she just needs ONE reliable reference note and she can tune the whole banjo in 30 seconds. But every tool gives her confusing visual displays or apps that bombard her with ads she's terrified of clicking. "I was charge nurse on a cardiac unit. I just don't have a mental map for how these things work."
- **Daily friction:** Arthritis limits practice to 15-30 minutes before pain sets in. Every minute spent wrestling with a tuner is stolen from playing — and from the arthritis window that's already shrinking. Her fingers don't grip tuning pegs as well as they used to, turning a 20-second tune-up into a 60-second one. Technology friction compounds physical friction.
- **What keeps her up at night:** That her declining eyesight, stiffening fingers, and unfamiliarity with technology will force her to give up playing — and with it, her weekly social connection. "If I couldn't tune, if I couldn't play, if I had to drop out... I don't want to think about that."
- **Previous failed attempts:** Pitch pipe (worked, but lost it in a move — Earl bought it, God rest him). Snark clip-on ("The letters changed so fast I couldn't read them. I felt stupid, and I'm not stupid"). Free tuner app (accidentally clicked an ad, downloaded something, panicked, called Colleen to "clean it up," never opened the app again).

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Arrives 20 minutes early to rehearsal and asks Marcus the pianist to play C-G-B-D so she can tune by ear — a skill she's had for decades. "The early bird tuning club" — Robert the trombonist does the same. She tunes better with her hearing aid removed because it distorts the banjo's high frequencies (~85dB at 2.5 feet overloads hearing aid circuitry). Phone speaker clarity at close range matters more than raw volume.
- **Tools they use:** Old Samsung Galaxy phone, printed songbook, physical metronome. She is not online.
- **Where they spend time online:** She is not online. Acquisition must happen through Colleen, bandmates, or potentially her doctor/occupational therapist. The social prescribing channel (healthcare providers recommending music tools) is emerging and directly relevant.
- **Decision triggers:** If Colleen physically installs the app and shows her how to use it. If a bandmate says "Betty, try this." If her occupational therapist says "there's an app that plays the notes for you." The app must be explainable in one sentence: "Press the button, hear the note, tune to match."

### A Day in Their Life
Betty wakes at 7am, takes her arthritis medication, and waits for it to kick in. Around 10am she picks up her plectrum banjo — 20 minutes is all her hands give her these days. She strums a chord and winces — the 2nd string sounds off. She picks up the phone, presses the side button like Colleen showed her, swipes up, and squints at the little squares. She finds what she thinks is the tuner app, taps it, and banners appear everywhere. Her instinct is to not touch anything — "last time I touched the wrong thing I ended up downloading something." She freezes, puts the phone down, and picks up the banjo again knowing it's out of tune. She plays for 12 minutes feeling frustrated, not because of the music, but because of the tuning. She resolves to get to rehearsal early Thursday and have Marcus help — the same resolution she makes every week.

### Interview Insights
1. **The tuning problem is also a GRIEF problem.** Earl, her late husband, used to play guitar. "When we'd practice together in the living room, he'd give me my starting notes. He'd just play the chord and I'd tune to it. It was part of our routine for twenty-some years." The app literally replaces the person she lost. This is not a tech problem — it's an emotional wound that technology could quietly salve.
2. **The band is literally her healthcare.** Her doctor recommended "staying active with music" after a mild depression screening. "Some weeks that Tuesday rehearsal is the only reason I get dressed up and leave the house." Social prescribing is the clinical term for what her Tuesday band rehearsal provides.
3. **She tunes BETTER with her hearing aid removed.** The hearing aid distorts the banjo's high frequencies. She hears more clearly with the aid out and the banjo close to her ear. Phone speaker clarity at close range matters more than raw volume — this is a counterintuitive design requirement.
4. **Fear of accidental purchase is not irrational — it's learned from real experience.** She once accidentally clicked an ad, downloaded something, panicked, and called Colleen to fix it. "My daughter told me never to tap on ads because they charge your credit card." Every ad near a button is a threat. This fear is clinically documented across the 65+ demographic and is reinforced by real negative experiences.
5. **She has a concrete mental model for trust.** "If it worked the first time Colleen put it on my phone, and it worked the second time I opened it by myself, and nothing scary happened either time." Two successful uses with zero scary moments. That's the conversion bar — low in theory, unmet in practice.

### In Their Own Words
> "I was charge nurse on a cardiac unit. I just don't have a mental map for how these things work, and every bad experience makes me less willing to try again."
*Source: Betty L. interview, Q7*

> "After he passed, I didn't have that anymore, and I'll be honest with you, the tuning problem is partly a grief problem. It's a reminder that my partner isn't there to do that small thing anymore."
*Source: Betty L. interview, Q10*

> "I would only recommend something if I felt like it respected me. Not respected me like a customer — respected me like a person who knows what she's doing musically but just needs a little help with the technology part."
*Source: Betty L. interview, Q9*

### Product Fit
- **Why she'd choose this:** Big buttons that play the actual sound. Matches how she's tuned for 40 years — by ear from a reference note. Looping playback lets her tune with both hands free ("I can't hold the phone and turn a tuning peg at the same time. My hands don't cooperate like that anymore"). Her pitch discrimination improves with longer tone exposure, making continuous loops ideal. Warm tone quality matters — "not a robot beep."
- **Why she'd walk away:** If the tuning is DGBD and not CGBD — literally the wrong notes. If ads appear near the buttons — a single accidental click means permanent deletion and reinforced avoidance. If touch targets are too small for arthritic fingers ("big enough that my arthritic fingers can tap them without hitting the wrong one"). If it has hamburger menus, gear icons, settings screens, or anything that requires a mental map she doesn't have. If it asks for permissions she doesn't understand.
- **Willingness to pay:** $5-10 if Colleen sets up the purchase. "That's cheaper than one visit to the pharmacy." Compares to the free pianist (which costs her pride) and the pitch pipe Earl bought her (which she lost in a move). One-time purchase only — subscriptions are incomprehensible.

**Surprise:** The guitar player Steve in her band already uses an app that plays reference tones — exactly what she wants — but he told her it was "for guitar" and didn't think it did banjo. The solution she needs exists in concept; it just hasn't reached her in a form she can access. The gap is not technology — it's the last ten feet of distribution to a 72-year-old woman who doesn't go to app stores.

## Persona 4: Rafael S., 38
*Sao Paulo-born cavaquinho player in a Brooklyn choro roda who needs DGBD reference tones but would never in his life search for "banjo tuner"*

**Location:** Astoria, Queens, NYC (Brazilian diaspora community)
**Income:** $52,000/year (restaurant manager + weekend gig musician)
**Tech savviness:** 3/5 — Comfortable with WhatsApp, YouTube, Instagram. Uses apps in Portuguese when available. Not a power user but not afraid of technology.
**Decision authority:** Full. Makes quick decisions on free/cheap tools. Shares recommendations instantly via WhatsApp group with 40+ Brazilian musicians in the NYC metro area.

### Goals
- **Primary:** Keep his cavaquinho in standard DGBD tuning for the Saturday choro roda at a Brazilian restaurant in Astoria, plus occasional samba gigs at festivals
- **Secondary:** Have a tuning tool that works quickly between sets when the restaurant is loud with conversation, clinking glasses, and kitchen noise
- **Hidden:** He wants to be taken seriously as a musician in the NYC Brazilian scene. Being in tune is professionalism made audible. "Being out of tune in a choro roda is like showing up to a job interview with your shirt untucked." But deeper than personal pride: "When I'm in tune and the roda is flowing — everyone locked in, the melodies interweaving — that's the best feeling in music."

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** The dedicated Cavaquinho Tuner app — his primary tool — is built by one person in Brazil and is deteriorating. It crashes, freezes on the splash screen, and the developer has stopped responding to bug reports. Generic chromatic tuners don't know what a cavaquinho is and require manual D3-G3-B3-D4 selection every time.
- **Daily friction:** Every gig is in a loud environment — restaurants, street festivals, parties. Mic-based tuners are completely useless. Clip-on tuners pick up "the TV above the bar, conversations, someone's kid crying." He needs to HEAR a reference tone, not show a microphone a sound. Setup time is the second friction: generic tuners require re-configuring each note every session because they don't remember he plays cavaquinho.
- **What keeps him up at night:** That the cavaquinho will remain invisible to the American tech ecosystem. "Every music app defaults to guitar. My instrument doesn't exist in the dropdown menu of any major tuner app."
- **Previous failed attempts:** The Cavaquinho Tuner app (good when it works, increasingly buggy and unmaintained), GuitarTuna (cavaquinho not supported, banjo behind paywall, banjo is 5-string anyway), clip-on Snark (works in quiet rooms, useless at gigs), tuning from the violao player's strings (works but feels like beginner dependency).

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** The Cavaquinho Tuner app is his primary tool, falling back to the violao player when it crashes. His friend Thiago keeps a voice memo of himself playing each open string in tune and plays it back to tune — "creative but kind of sad that it's the best solution he found."
- **Tools they use:** Samsung Galaxy A54, WhatsApp (primary communication — "in the Brazilian community, WhatsApp IS the internet"), YouTube for choro tutorials and performances, Instagram for promoting gigs, the Cavaquinho Tuner app, a simple metronome app, Spotify
- **Where they spend time online:** WhatsApp groups (40+ musician group for NYC Brazilian scene, smaller choro roda group), YouTube, Instagram. Searches in Portuguese first, always: "afinador de cavaquinho." Would never visit Banjo Hangout or search "banjo tuner."
- **Decision triggers:** If someone in his WhatsApp group shares a link. If a YouTube choro tutorial mentions a tuner app. If he sees "cavaquinho" in an app store listing while searching "afinador de cavaquinho." Word of mouth through WhatsApp is how almost everything spreads: "One share in the right group and suddenly two hundred people know about it."

### A Day in Their Life
Rafael finishes his Saturday lunch shift at the restaurant at 3pm. The choro roda starts at 7pm in the back room. He arrives at 6:30, pulls out his cavaquinho, and opens the Cavaquinho Tuner app. It freezes on the splash screen. Again. He closes it, opens it again — same thing. The kitchen is already going full blast. He opens a generic chromatic tuner, manually selects D, plucks the string — the app picks up a waiter dropping a plate and the needle goes haywire. He waits for Marcos the violao player to arrive so he can tune off his strings. It works, but it took five minutes he didn't have, and he feels embarrassed — he's been playing since he was fourteen in Sao Paulo.

### Interview Insights
1. **WhatsApp distribution creates cascade effects.** One share in his 40+ musician group reaches 15+ cavaquinho/bandolim players. Those players share in their groups. "That's how things spread in the Brazilian community — it's not app store reviews, it's not Twitter, it's WhatsApp." This is a viral distribution mechanism with near-zero customer acquisition cost.
2. **Cavaquinho players number in the MILLIONS in Brazil alone.** "Samba, pagode, choro — cavaquinho is in all of them." The diaspora is global: NYC, Boston, Miami, Newark, Japan, Portugal, all over Europe. Every city with a Brazilian community has cavaquinho players with the same tuning problem. The market dwarfs the 4-string banjo market.
3. **The app's DGBD tuning is already a perfect technical match — zero code changes needed.** The entire barrier is discovery and cultural framing. Rafael searches "afinador de cavaquinho" in Portuguese. Banjen doesn't appear. "Even though the tuning is the same, the cultural connection isn't there. If you told me a banjo app would work for my cavaquinho, I might try it, but I would never discover it on my own."
4. **He wants auto-advance through strings.** "An app that plays all four strings in sequence automatically — D, then G, then B, then D — with maybe a five-second gap between each one." This is independently the same "session mode" that Siobhan described — convergent design evidence from two unrelated personas.
