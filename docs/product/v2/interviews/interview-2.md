# Interview: Siobhan K., 34
*Irish traditional musician, tenor banjo, weekly pub sessions*

---

**Q1: Walk me through the last time you had to tune your banjo in a pub session. What happened step by step?**

A1: Right, so this was Tuesday night at Hughes' — the regular session. We'd just finished a set of reels and I could feel my D string had gone flat, which happens constantly because the radiator is blasting right next to where I sit. I had maybe 25 seconds before Pádraig counted in the next set. So I'm rooting around in my case pocket for the tuning fork — naturally it's slipped down through the gap in the lining again, I swear that case eats them — and by the time I find it, strike it on my knee, and get it to my ear, the set has started. So I just... didn't play the first tune. Sat there like a lemon. Then between the first and second tune in the set, I got the A string sorted from the fork and quickly tuned the D off it by fifths. But the G and E I just had to leave and hope for the best. To be fair, the E was grand, but the G was noticeably off and I was self-conscious about it the whole set. It's mortifying when you know you're slightly out and everyone can hear it but nobody says anything.

---

**Q2: What's the most frustrating part of how you handle tuning today?**

A2: Look, the honest answer is that there's no single tool that just *works* for what I need. I play tenor banjo — GDAE — and the entire tuner app market acts like that doesn't exist. Every app I open defaults to EADGBE or open G, and I'm there scrolling through menus trying to find something that matches my tuning, and by then the moment's gone. My tuning forks actually work — acoustically they're perfect, they cut through pub noise, no battery, no UI — but I keep losing the bloody things. I'm on my fourth A fork this year. They fall out of pockets, roll under tables, end up in the hoover at school. The frustration isn't really about the *tuning* itself. It's about the 15 seconds of fumbling and searching before you can even start tuning. In a session, those 15 seconds are everything.

---

**Q3: What have you tried before? Why did it stop working?**

A3: Oh God, I've been through the lot. Cleartune was the first — lovely app in a quiet room, completely useless in Whelan's on a Friday when there's a fiddle, a flute, a bodhrán, and forty people talking. The microphone just picks up everything. I tried clip-on tuners — the Snark and then a TC Electronic one — and they're better, but in a session the vibrations from the table travel through the clip and give you phantom readings. Plus I looked ridiculous with a tuner clipped to my headstock while everyone else just *tunes*. PitchLab I downloaded because someone on The Session recommended it, but the interface is like a cockpit instrument panel. I spent ten minutes trying to find the reference tone feature and gave up. The tuning forks genuinely work the best of anything I've tried — the problem is purely physical. They're small, they're metal, they roll, and they disappear. I've taped one to the inside of my case before but the tape residue drove me mad. So now I just accept I'll buy a new fork every couple of months. It's daft.

---

**Q4: If you found a new app that claimed to solve this — what's the first thing that would make you trust it? What's the first thing that would make you close it immediately?**

A4: Trust — if I opened it and GDAE was right there. Not buried in a settings menu, not "custom tuning," not requiring me to manually enter frequencies. Just... tenor banjo, GDAE, there it is. That tells me the developer actually knows my instrument exists. That's surprisingly rare. If a session player I respect — someone like Eoin on The Session or Ciarán from my Wednesday group — posted about it, I'd download it immediately. I trust people who play over people who code, if that makes sense. What would make me close it? Ah sure, a full-screen ad before I can do anything. Instantly gone. Or if it opens and the first thing I see is a 5-string banjo graphic. That tells me this app isn't for me, it's for American bluegrass players, and I'm an afterthought. Also, any app that requires me to create an account or sign in just to play a reference tone — deleted. I need this to work in three seconds or less. No onboarding, no tutorials, no "rate us" popups.

---

**Q5: What would you pay for a perfect solution? What are you comparing that to?**

A5: For an app that genuinely solved this? That had GDAE built in, played reference tones loud enough through my earbuds to hear over session noise, and opened instantly? I'd pay four or five euro without thinking twice. I spend more than that on a pint. I'm comparing it to the cost of tuning forks — they're about six euro each and I go through maybe four a year, so that's twenty-four euro annually on things I keep losing. But honestly, the real comparison is the cost of *not* having it, which is sitting out a set because you couldn't tune fast enough. That's priceless, like. The thing is, I'd also happily pay to remove ads. If an app is free with ads, I'll tolerate small banner ads but the moment a video ad plays while I'm trying to tune mid-session, that app is dead to me. I'd rather pay upfront than deal with that anxiety.

---

**Q6: Who else at sessions deals with this? How do they handle it differently?**

A6: Nearly everyone who plays a fretted instrument. The fiddle players have it easier — they tune by fifths from memory and they're at it since they were children, so it's second nature. But the other tenor banjo players, the mandolin players, the bouzouki players — we're all in the same boat. Pádraig uses a clip-on but he's forever fiddling with it and squinting at it in the dark. Ciarán has perfect pitch, the jammy bastard, so he just *knows*. The rest of us either use forks, tune off each other, or tune off the fiddle player's A string, which is grand if she's in tune herself. The younger lads tend to use apps with earbuds in — I've seen a few using guitar tuner apps and just ignoring the strings they don't need, which is exactly the humiliating workaround I used to do. There's about eight of us in the Tuesday session who play tenor banjo or octave mandolin — all GDAE — and not one of us has a dedicated tool. We all cobble something together. If something actually worked, I'd tell every one of them.

---

**Q7: Show me your phone — what music apps do you use regularly?**

A7: Right, let me pull it up. So I've got Tunefox — I use that for learning new tunes, it's brilliant for slowing things down and looping sections. The Session app — well, it's actually just a Safari bookmark to thesession.org, but I use it constantly to look up tune settings and find what's being played at different sessions around Dublin. I've got OAIM — that's the Online Academy of Irish Music — where I do a masterclass with Martin Hayes's method. WhatsApp obviously, for the session group chats. Voice Memos — I record bits of sessions so I can learn tunes by ear later. Spotify for listening to recordings of tunes I'm working on. And then there's a metronome app, Pro Metronome, which I use when practicing at home. I *had* Cleartune installed for about six months but I deleted it after it was useless at the third session in a row. No dedicated tuner app on my phone right now. I genuinely use my physical tuning fork more than any app for tuning.

---

**Q8: What's a feature you'd love that you've never seen anyone build for a tuner app?**

A8: Oh, this is a good one. I'd love a "session mode" where you set your tuning — GDAE for me — and it just cycles through the strings automatically. Like, it plays G for five seconds, then moves to D, then A, then E, with maybe a little vibration or flash between each one so you know it's moving on even if you can't see the screen. Because in a session, my phone is face-down on the table with one earbud in. I can't be looking at the screen and tapping buttons. I need to hear the reference tone, tune that string, and have the next one come automatically. Nobody's built that. Every tuner app assumes you're sitting in a quiet room staring at your phone. The other thing — and this is pure fantasy — would be if it could detect when a string is in tune through the earbud mic and automatically advance. But honestly, the simple auto-advance with a timer would transform how I tune at sessions. Five seconds per string, twenty seconds total, done.

---

**Q9: What would make you recommend this to your session mates? How would you do it?**

A9: If it worked. That's it. If I used it at a session and it actually solved the problem — I tuned quickly, I didn't miss a set, it was painless — I'd tell people that night. "Here, look at this." That's how things spread in sessions. Someone pulls out a thing, it works, and by next week three other people have it. I wouldn't write a review or post on social media about it — I'd literally show Pádraig and Ciarán and Maeve at the table between sets. If it was really good, I'd post about it on The Session, which is where every Irish traditional musician goes. A recommendation there from someone with a decent post history carries enormous weight. Hundreds of lurkers, thousands of session players worldwide. One thread called "Finally a tuner that works for tenor banjo" and you'd have downloads from Dublin to Boston to Melbourne overnight. But I'd only post if I was genuinely impressed. My reputation in that community matters more than any app. I'd also mention it in the IrishBanjoLessons Discord, which has about 400 active members, mostly GDAE players.

---

**Q10: What am I not asking that I should be?**

A10: You should be asking about octave mandolin. Everyone focuses on "banjo" but a huge number of Irish session players double on octave mandolin — same GDAE tuning, different instrument. I switch between the two depending on the session and the repertoire. Any tool that works for tenor banjo GDAE works identically for octave mandolin. You're not selling to one instrument, you're selling to an entire tuning system. Also — you should ask about the social dynamics of tuning at sessions. There's an unspoken thing where if you're seen struggling with technology, it undermines your credibility as a musician. The best players tune quickly and invisibly. Any tool that draws attention to itself — a bright screen, a loud notification, anything that makes someone look at you and think "she's using an app" — that's actually worse than being slightly out of tune. The tool needs to be invisible. Phone face-down, one earbud, done in twenty seconds, nobody notices. That's the standard. And finally — you should ask about offline use. Not every pub in rural Ireland has reliable mobile data, and I don't want an app that needs to phone home to play me a reference tone.
