# Interview: Harold M., 67
*Retired accountant returning to music after 50 years*

---

**Q1: Walk me through the last time you ran into trouble tuning your banjo. What happened step by step?**

A1: Well, it was Tuesday morning — I do my practice at seven after coffee, same time every day. I picked up my Goodtime and it just sounded off, you know, kind of sour. So I grabbed my phone and opened YouTube, typed in "D G B D banjo reference tone." First result had a thirty-second ad for some truck dealership. I waited through that, and then the video started but the fellow was talking for two minutes before he even played a note. When he finally did, my wife Linda was running the dishwasher in the kitchen, and I couldn't tell if my string matched or not. I tried turning the peg a little, plucked again, but the video had already moved on to the next string. I rewound it, got another ad. The way I see it, I spent fifteen minutes fighting with my phone and maybe forty-five seconds actually listening to reference tones. I just gave up and played my exercises knowing it probably sounded wrong. It's like trying to balance a ledger with half the numbers missing — you know something's off but you can't pin it down.

---

**Q2: What's the most frustrating part of how you handle this today?**

A2: To be honest with you, the most frustrating part is the dependence. I wait for my Thursday lesson with Ray — that's my teacher — and I ask him to tune it for me at the start. That eats five or ten minutes of a lesson I'm paying forty dollars for. And then I'm afraid to play too aggressively between lessons because I might knock it out of tune again. So I'm babying the instrument instead of learning it. It's like — imagine you had a car but you could only get the tires checked once a week. You'd drive real careful, wouldn't you? That's what I'm doing. I'm not practicing with confidence. And at my age, to be frank, I don't have unlimited time to waste on a problem that shouldn't be this hard.

---

**Q3: What have you tried before? Why did it stop working for you?**

A3: Oh, I've been through the wringer. My daughter-in-law downloaded GuitarTuna on my phone last Christmas. Looked real nice at first, but when I tried to switch to banjo tuning it wanted me to pay — I think it was ten dollars a year or something. I'm not opposed to paying, but I hadn't even tried it yet and it's already got its hand out. Before that, Ray sold me a Snark clip-on tuner. The little needle jumps around like a cat on a hot tin roof. I'd pluck the string and the needle would bounce from green to red and back, and I'd have no idea if I was getting closer or further away. I don't have the patience for that kind of guesswork. I also tried a free chromatic tuner app — I don't remember the name — but it just showed letters and cents, and I didn't know what note I was supposed to be aiming for on each string. It assumed I already knew things I didn't know. None of them were built for someone like me, the way I see it.

---

**Q4: If you found a new app that claimed to solve this — what's the first thing that would make you trust it? What's the first thing that would make you close it immediately?**

A4: Trust — if Ray told me about it. If my banjo teacher said "Harold, put this on your phone," I'd do it that afternoon. Short of that, I'd want to open it and immediately understand what I'm looking at. No sign-up screen, no tutorial I have to swipe through, no asking for my email. Just — here are your four strings, press one, hear the tone. That's trust to me: the app respects that I have a banjo in my lap and I just need a sound. What would make me close it? Ads. A big flashing ad before I've even done anything. Or one of those ads that takes over the whole screen and you can't find the tiny X to close it. I'd be gone in two seconds. I don't mind a small ad at the bottom — I understand people have to make a living — but if it gets between me and the reason I opened the app, that's a dealbreaker. Also, if it asked me to create an account. I'm tuning a banjo, not applying for a mortgage.

---

**Q5: What would you pay for a perfect solution? What are you comparing that to?**

A5: That's a good question. The way I see it, I paid eighteen dollars for my Snark clip-on and it sits in a drawer. I pay Ray forty dollars an hour and I waste ten minutes of every lesson on tuning. So the tuning problem is costing me — let me think — about six or seven dollars a lesson in wasted time, times four lessons a month, that's twenty-five, thirty dollars a month in a way. For an app that just worked, every time, no fuss? I'd pay three, maybe five dollars one time. I compare everything to a physical object. Five dollars is a set of banjo strings. Five dollars is a cup of coffee and a donut at the place Linda and I go on Saturdays. That feels about right for a tool I'd use every single day. A subscription though? No. I'm retired on a fixed income. I'm not signing up for another monthly bill. I've got enough of those.

---

**Q6: Who else in your life deals with this? How do they handle it differently than you?**

A6: There's a fellow at the jam circle, Don, he's been playing about four years. He's got one of those clip-on tuners and he's fast with it — I've watched him tune up in about ninety seconds. But he told me it took him a year to get comfortable reading it. A year! I don't want to spend a year learning to use a tuner. There's also a younger woman, Kathy, maybe forty-five, she uses some app on her iPhone — I think it listens to the string and tells her if she's sharp or flat. She seems to like it fine, but she's also a lot more comfortable with technology than I am. My grandson Tyler is fifteen and plays guitar, and he tunes by ear somehow, just knows when it sounds right. I played trumpet in the marching band fifty years ago, and I think I used to have a little bit of that ability, but it's been dormant a long time. To be honest, I sometimes wonder if I could get it back if I just had a reliable reference tone to practice against.

---

**Q7: Show me your phone — what apps do you use for anything related to music or banjo?**

A7: Let me get it here. Alright, well, there's YouTube — that's where I watch Jim Pankey's beginner lessons, he's real good. There's that GuitarTuna that my daughter-in-law put on, but I haven't opened it since it asked me to pay. There's the Samsung voice recorder — I record myself playing sometimes so I can listen back, which is actually kind of painful but useful. And that's really it for music. I've got my regular stuff — phone, texts, weather, Facebook, the calculator. Oh, and Google — I Google things like "how to tune a banjo" probably once a week and end up on the same unhelpful pages. I don't have a lot of apps. Linda jokes that my phone is the most expensive calculator in Knoxville. The way I see it, every app on there is one I've been walked through by someone I trust, or it's something that came with the phone.

---

**Q8: What's a feature you'd love that you've never seen anyone build for a tuner app?**

A8: You know what I've never seen? Something that teaches me to hear the difference. Not just plays the note — actually helps me train my ear so that eventually I don't need the app at all. Like, play the reference tone, then let me pluck my string, and tell me "you're close" or "way off, tighten that peg." Almost like a patient teacher sitting next to me. Because the goal isn't to be dependent on a phone forever — the goal is to get good enough that I can hear it myself. I had that sense once, back in the trumpet days. I remember Mr. Halverson, our band director, he'd play an A on the piano and we'd all tune to it. That's all I need, really — a patient, reliable A on the piano, except it's D-G-B-D on a banjo. And maybe something that notices I'm getting better over time, like a little encouragement. "Harold, you're tuning faster this week." That would mean a lot to an old man sitting alone with his banjo at seven in the morning.

---

**Q9: What would make you recommend this to someone else in your jam circle?**

A9: If I could show them in ten seconds. I mean that literally — if I could pull out my phone at the jam circle, tap one button, hear the tone, tune my string, and have Don or Kathy say "what's that app?" That's how recommendations work in our group. Nobody's reading app store reviews. It's all "what are you using?" If it's simple enough that I — Harold, the guy who can barely find the camera app — if I can use it without help, that's the strongest endorsement I could give. I'd say "my grandson didn't even have to set it up for me." That would get people's attention. Also, honestly, if it worked without Wi-Fi. We play at the community center and the Wi-Fi there is terrible. If the app needed internet to work I couldn't use it at the jam, and that's where I need it most besides my morning practice.

---

**Q10: What am I not asking that I should be?**

A10: Well — and I'm a little embarrassed to bring this up — you haven't asked me why I'm really doing this. It's not just the jam circle. I read an article in the AARP magazine about how learning music helps keep your brain sharp as you get older. Linda's mother had dementia, and I watched what that did to the family. I haven't told Linda this is part of why I practice every morning. She thinks it's just a hobby. But when I sit down with that banjo at seven AM, part of what I'm doing is — well, exercising my brain. The discipline, the finger patterns, the listening. So when something as basic as tuning creates a barrier, it's not just annoying. It's threatening the whole routine. If I get frustrated enough to skip a day, then two days, then a week — that scares me more than sounding bad at the jam. You should be asking what's really at stake for people. It's usually more than they'll tell you upfront. For me, it's staying sharp. Staying myself. The banjo is just the vehicle.
