### Goal-Based User Clusters

1. **"Just get me in tune so I can play"** — Harold, Betty, Rafael. They want reliability, not features. The tuning experience is overhead to minimize.
2. **"Let me tune fast and discreetly in a live setting"** — Siobhan, session players. Speed and stealth in noisy environments are the priority.
3. **"Don't make me configure anything"** — Marcus, TikTok discoverers. Instant utility with zero setup. If it takes more than 10 seconds, they leave.
4. **"See me — my instrument exists"** — Betty (plectrum), Siobhan (Irish tenor), Rafael (cavaquinho). These users feel invisible to an industry that defaults to 5-string banjo and guitar.

## Persona 1: Harold M., 67
*Retired accountant returning to music after 50 years, using banjo practice as a daily cognitive health routine*

**Location:** Suburban Knoxville, Tennessee
**Income:** $55,000/year (pension + Social Security)
**Tech savviness:** 2/5 — Uses smartphone for calls, texts, weather, YouTube. Grandkids set up new apps. Comfortable with repetitive workflows once learned; anxious about anything unfamiliar.
**Decision authority:** Full. Spends his own money, no one to convince. But cautious — compares every app purchase to a physical product ("Is this worth more than a set of strings?").
**Research Evidence:** Market-analyst (retirement-age first-time players, STAM model), Community-listener (YouTube reference tone workaround), Social-analyst (Cognitive Warrior segment, "determination despite doubt" emotional tone, Bogleheads thread), Academic-researcher (Lapsed Musician Returner archetype, neuroprotective hobbyist, technology anxiety, self-determination theory)

### Goals
- **Primary:** Learn to play banjo well enough to join the Thursday evening bluegrass jam circle at the community center
- **Secondary:** Maintain a structured daily routine that replaces the discipline of his 35-year accounting career
- **Hidden:** He read an AARP article about music and brain health and hasn't told anyone, but the neuroprotective angle is quietly his strongest motivator. He fears cognitive decline more than he fears sounding bad.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** Cannot tell if his banjo is in tune. His ear is untrained — or so he thinks. (Research suggests he may have dormant aural skills from playing trumpet in his high school marching band 50 years ago, but he's forgotten he has them.)
- **Daily friction:** Every tuner app he's tried has confusing interfaces, too many options, or aggressive ads that interrupt the simple act of tuning. YouTube tuning videos start with 30-second unskippable ads. He spends more time fighting technology than playing.
- **What keeps him up at night:** Fear that he's "too old to learn" and will waste money on an instrument that collects dust. A Banjo Hangout user was told "it would be a 20-year learning curve" — Harold has internalized this kind of discouragement even though community members fiercely reject it.
- **Previous failed attempts:** Downloaded GuitarTuna (required in-app purchase for banjo), tried a Snark clip-on (readings were jumpy and confusing), gave up on a generic chromatic tuner because he didn't know which notes to target. Tried searching "D B G D banjo reference tone" on YouTube and got an ad-laden, noisy experience.

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Asks his banjo teacher to tune it at weekly lessons, then avoids playing too much between lessons so it stays "mostly in tune." Also searches YouTube for DGBD reference tone videos — a clunky workaround that is literally what Banjen replaces.
- **Tools they use:** Samsung Galaxy phone (2 years old), YouTube for banjo lessons, a spiral notebook for tab, physical metronome
- **Where they spend time online:** YouTube (85% of adults 50-64 use it — Pew 2025), Facebook (74% of adults 50-64), Banjo Hangout (reads but rarely posts), local Facebook group for his jam circle
- **Decision triggers:** If his banjo teacher specifically recommends an app by name. If a friend at the jam says "here, try this." Teacher recommendations are the #1 acquisition channel for this demographic.

### A Day in Their Life
Harold wakes at 6:30, has coffee, and reads an article about how learning a musical instrument protects against dementia. He picks up his Deering Goodtime for 30 minutes of practice. He starts by trying to tune — opens YouTube, watches a 30-second ad, then struggles to hear the reference over the kitchen noise. He gives up fine-tuning, plays through his lesson exercises suspecting it sounds "a little off," and resolves to ask his teacher on Thursday. The frustration slightly dims his quiet pride in showing up every morning.

### In Their Own Words
> "All of the online tuners seem to be caught in that programming vortex. I just want something that plays the note so I can match it."
*Source: Market-analyst report — SteveZ, Banjo Hangout thread #284504*

> "One can buy education, but one absolutely cannot buy wisdom. A recent study found that cognitive decline does not necessarily correlate to aging. It does however correlate to not pursuing new skills."
*Source: Social-analyst report — 35planar, Banjo Hangout "You're too old" thread*

### Product Fit
- **Why he'd choose this:** Plays the actual tone. He can hear it and match it — no screen to interpret, no confusing needle, no letter names he doesn't know. Four buttons, four strings, picture of a banjo. "String 1, String 2, String 3, String 4" would be ideal. Research shows reducing choices from 24 to 6 increases completion from 3% to 30% (Schwartz paradox of choice) — Banjen's 4 buttons eliminate the choice problem entirely.
- **Why he'd walk away:** If ads interrupt the tuning flow — a single bad ad experience causes permanent deletion in this demographic. If the interface uses music terminology he doesn't understand (he doesn't know string names). If there's any perception of subscription pricing or accidental purchase risk.
- **Willingness to pay:** $2-5 one-time purchase to remove ads. Compares it to the $15 Snark clip-on he already bought and the $5 tuning fork set at the music shop. Subscriptions are an absolute dealbreaker — research confirms older adults prefer one-time purchases they understand.

## Persona 2: Siobhan K., 34
*Irish traditional musician who plays tenor banjo in weekly pub sessions and represents a dense, interconnected ecosystem of GDAE players*

**Location:** Dublin, Ireland (uses English-language apps; sessions in pubs with poor cell reception)
**Income:** EUR 42,000/year (primary school teacher)
**Tech savviness:** 4/5 — Comfortable with technology, uses multiple music apps, participates in a Discord community for Irish banjo students. But in sessions, wants zero tech friction.
**Decision authority:** Full. Makes small purchases without hesitation. More importantly, she's a **recommendation multiplier** — one endorsement from her reaches 20+ session players in her network.
**Research Evidence:** Market-analyst (Irish tenor banjo ecosystem, Willie Clancy, teacher distribution channel), Community-listener (Fifths Family multi-instrumentalist, online teachers as kingmakers), Social-analyst (Irish tenor banjo educators, Quiet Session Player lurker segment), Competitor-analyst (only Ultimate Banjo Tuner has GDAE on Android, ad-heavy)

### Goals
- **Primary:** Stay perfectly in tune during fast-paced pub sessions where stopping to re-tune is socially unacceptable — she has 20-30 seconds between tune sets
- **Secondary:** Have a single reference-tone tool that works for her tenor banjo AND her octave mandolin (both GDAE), replacing the tuning forks she keeps losing
- **Hidden:** She wants to be the person who discovers and recommends the perfect tool to her session network. Being a trusted voice on gear and tools is part of her musical identity.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** Most banjo tuner apps assume 5-string open G. GDAE (Irish tenor) is rarely a default and sometimes not available at all. She currently uses "a guitar tuner app and ignores the first/last tuning points" — a degrading workaround for a skilled musician.
- **Daily friction:** Pub environments are too noisy for mic-based tuners. Clip-on tuners pick up the fiddle player next to her. She needs reference tones through earbuds — the ONLY approach that works at sessions.
- **What keeps her up at night:** Showing up to a session with a banjo that drifts out of tune during a fast reel set and not being able to fix it quickly. "If you're fumbling with an app while everyone is starting the next set, you just don't play that set. And if that happens twice, people notice."
- **Previous failed attempts:** Cleartune (accurate but useless in noisy pubs), PitchLab (overwhelming UI for quick session use), tuning forks (silent but easy to lose, requires a quiet moment), guitar tuner apps (humiliating workaround).

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Tuning forks she keeps in her banjo case. She tunes one string (A or D) from the fork and derives the rest by fifths — a standard skill among trad players. She doesn't need all four strings played for her; she needs ONE reliable reference pitch.
- **Tools they use:** iPhone 14, Tunefox for learning tunes, The Session website, WhatsApp group for session scheduling, IrishBanjoLessons.com Discord
- **Where they spend time online:** The Session (thesession.org — the primary online hub), Irish music Facebook groups, Comhaltas forums, OAIM (oaim.ie). For every active poster on The Session, there are 10-50 silent readers who follow recommendations without asking.
- **Decision triggers:** If another trusted session player recommends it, or if she sees it mentioned positively on The Session. One strategic post by a respected user on The Session could trigger silent adoption across hundreds of lurkers. If Enda Scahill or Lisa Canny (Irish banjo teachers with structured online courses) endorsed it, she'd install immediately.

### A Day in Their Life
Siobhan finishes school at 3pm, grabs her banjo case and her octave mandolin bag, and heads to the pub for the Tuesday session. She arrives 15 minutes early. The pub is already loud with conversation and a football match on the TV. She opens her case, digs for a tuning fork, can't find the A — it slipped between the lining. She holds her phone to her ear with a guitar tuner app set to A440, tunes the A string, then tunes D, G, E by fifths. It takes 4 minutes and she feels annoyed that in 2026 no app just plays GDAE tones.

### In Their Own Words
> "A chromatic tuner simply won't work for fiddle. End of story. The same goes for banjo in a session."
*Source: Community-listener report — Will Evans, The Session discussion #48723*

> "If someone made a proper 4-string tuner with Irish tuning as a default, I'd tell every session player I know. We're all using workarounds."
*Source: Existing ux-report.md — Siobhan K. interview*

### Product Fit
- **Why she'd choose this:** Reference-tone approach works in noisy pubs. Offline functionality is non-negotiable (bad pub WiFi). If GDAE were a first-class tuning preset, she'd switch instantly and become an evangelist through her session network, the Discord, and The Session forums.
- **Why she'd walk away:** If the app only supports DGBD and not GDAE Irish tenor tuning — she would never use a tuner that assumes plectrum/Chicago tuning. No GDAE = no install. Period.
- **Willingness to pay:** EUR 3-5 for an app with GDAE as a first-class option. Compares to the EUR 4 she paid for Cleartune and the EUR 8 set of tuning forks she keeps replacing. Would cheerfully pay to support a product that visibly serves the Irish trad community.

## Persona 3: Betty L., 72
*Retired nurse and 40-year plectrum banjo veteran whose declining eyesight and arthritis make technology a barrier to the music that keeps her socially alive*

**Location:** New Orleans, Louisiana
**Income:** $40,000/year (pension)
**Tech savviness:** 1/5 — Has an Android phone her daughter set up. Calls and texts only. Clinically documented technology anxiety: fear of accidental purchases, embarrassment at not understanding interfaces, avoidance spiral that prevents skill acquisition.
**Decision authority:** Full on small purchases, but relies on daughter or bandmate to install and demonstrate apps. Won't download apps she doesn't trust. Her daughter is the real gatekeeper.
**Research Evidence:** Academic-researcher (technology anxiety as primary barrier, fear of accidental purchase, social prescribing, NN/g senior UX guidelines, arthritis/dexterity research), Community-listener (arthritis and practice limits, hearing aids distort banjo, no accessible tuner on Android, plectrum players feel abandoned), Social-analyst (technology anxiety clinically documented, "I felt stupid and I'm not stupid" sentiment), Competitor-analyst (no app designed for older adults, accessibility as blue ocean)

### Goals
- **Primary:** Keep her plectrum banjo in standard CGBD tuning for weekly Dixieland band rehearsals — the band is her primary social lifeline since her husband passed
- **Secondary:** Maintain her independence as a musician; stop asking the trumpet player to tune her banjo
- **Hidden:** Her doctor suggested "staying active with music" after a mild depression screening — she'd never call it "social prescribing," but that's what it is. The band isn't just a hobby; it's her healthcare.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** She has 40 years of playing experience and excellent relative pitch — she just needs ONE reliable reference note and she can tune the whole banjo in 30 seconds. But every tool either gives her confusing visual displays she can't read, or apps that bombard her with ads she's terrified of clicking. "I felt stupid, and I'm not stupid."
- **Daily friction:** Arthritis in her hands means practice windows are 15-30 minutes before pain sets in. Every minute spent wrestling with a tuner is a minute stolen from playing. A 3-minute tuning struggle consumes 10-20% of her available practice time.
- **What keeps her up at night:** That her declining eyesight, stiffening fingers, and unfamiliarity with technology will force her to give up playing — and with it, her weekly social connection. Community music participation drives persistence and well-being in older adults; losing it correlates with depression and isolation (Music for Life project, N=398).
- **Previous failed attempts:** Snark clip-on tuner ("The letters changed so fast I couldn't read them. Sometimes it said C, sometimes C#, and I didn't know if I was going the right way"). A free tuner app (bombarded with ads, accidentally clicked one and downloaded something). She went back to arriving 20 minutes early to ask the pianist for reference notes.

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Arrives 20 minutes early to rehearsal and asks the pianist to play C-G-B-D so she can tune by ear — a skill she's had for decades. The app literally replaces the pianist. Her hearing aid sometimes distorts the banjo's high frequencies (~85dB at 2.5 feet overloads hearing aid circuitry), so she tunes better with the aid removed and the banjo close to her ear.
- **Tools they use:** Old Samsung Galaxy phone, printed songbook, physical metronome. She is not online. Her daughter sometimes shows her things on Facebook.
- **Where they spend time online:** She is not online. Acquisition must happen through her daughter, her bandmates, or potentially her doctor/occupational therapist. The social prescribing channel (healthcare providers recommending music tools) is emerging and directly relevant.
- **Decision triggers:** If her daughter physically installs the app and shows her how to use it. If a bandmate says "Betty, try this." If her occupational therapist says "there's an app that plays the notes for you." The app must be explainable in one sentence: "Press the button, hear the note, tune to match."

### A Day in Their Life
Betty wakes at 7am, takes her arthritis medication, and waits for it to kick in. Around 10am she picks up her plectrum banjo for 20 minutes of practice. She strums a chord and winces — the 2nd string sounds off. She tries adjusting it but her fingers ache on the tuning peg. She can't remember if B is higher or lower than what she's hearing. She gives up and decides she'll get to rehearsal early Thursday and have the pianist help. She puts the banjo down after 12 minutes feeling frustrated, not because of the music, but because of the tuning.

### In Their Own Words
> "The letters changed so fast I couldn't read them. I felt stupid, and I'm not stupid. Just play me the note and I'll get it."
*Source: Existing ux-report.md — Betty L. interview, corroborated by NN/g senior UX research and Pew 2017 technology anxiety data*

> "Don't try for marathon practice sessions, it'll just make your hands ache. Try for 15 to 30 minute sessions with rest in between."
*Source: Community-listener report — Banjo Hangout thread #364281 (Arthritis and the banjo)*

### Product Fit
- **Why she'd choose this:** Big buttons that play the actual sound she needs to hear. Matches how she's tuned for 40 years — by ear from a reference note. Looping playback lets her tune with both hands free. Her pitch discrimination improves with longer tone exposure (age-related hearing research confirms this), and Banjen's continuous loop is ideal.
- **Why she'd walk away:** If the tuning is DGBD and not CGBD (her plectrum standard) — literally the wrong notes. If ads appear near the buttons (fear of accidental purchase is clinically documented and widespread among 65+ users, not just her quirk). If touch targets are too small for arthritic fingers (research recommends 56-72dp for 65+ users, beyond Android's 48dp minimum). If she can't figure it out without help and her daughter isn't available.
- **Willingness to pay:** $3-5 if her daughter sets up the purchase. Compares to the $12 Snark that confused her and the $0 she pays the pianist. One-time purchase only — subscriptions are incomprehensible and frightening.

## Persona 4: Rafael S., 38
*São Paulo-born cavaquinho player in a Brooklyn choro roda who needs DGBD reference tones but would never in his life search for "banjo tuner"*

**Location:** Astoria, Queens, NYC (Brazilian diaspora community)
**Income:** $52,000/year (restaurant manager + weekend gig musician)
**Tech savviness:** 3/5 — Comfortable with WhatsApp, YouTube, Instagram. Uses apps in Portuguese when available. Not a power user but not afraid of technology.
**Decision authority:** Full. Makes quick decisions on free/cheap tools. Shares recommendations instantly via WhatsApp group with 40+ Brazilian musicians in the NYC metro area.
**Research Evidence:** Market-analyst (cavaquinho DGBD crossover, adjacent market), Community-listener (Brazilian Cavaquinho Player user type, TuCuatro, Cavaquinhonaut blog), Social-analyst (Cavaquinho Crossover segment, millions of players, Portuguese-language discovery, WhatsApp distribution)

### Goals
- **Primary:** Keep his cavaquinho in standard DGBD tuning for the Saturday choro roda at a Brazilian restaurant in Astoria, plus occasional samba gigs at festivals
- **Secondary:** Have a tuning tool that works quickly between sets when the restaurant is loud with conversation and clinking glasses
- **Hidden:** He wants to be taken seriously as a musician in the NYC Brazilian scene, which means always being in tune. Out-of-tune cavaquinho in a choro roda is like showing up to a job interview with your shirt untucked.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** The dedicated Cavaquinho Tuner app on Google Play works but is buggy and hasn't been updated recently. Generic chromatic tuners don't know what a cavaquinho is. He has to manually select D-G-B-D notes every time, which is annoying when he's setting up quickly before a gig.
- **Daily friction:** At restaurant gigs, the ambient noise (conversation, kitchen, music from the speaker system during breaks) makes mic-based tuners completely useless. He needs to HEAR a reference tone, not show a microphone a sound.
- **What keeps him up at night:** That the cavaquinho (and choro music generally) will remain invisible to the American tech ecosystem. Every music app defaults to guitar. His instrument doesn't exist in the dropdown menu of any major tuner app. He feels unseen.
- **Previous failed attempts:** The Cavaquinho Tuner app (works but buggy), GuitarTuna (cavaquinho not supported, banjo behind paywall), a clip-on tuner (picks up ambient noise at the restaurant), tuning from the violão (7-string guitar) player's open strings (works but he wants independence).

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Uses The Cavaquinho Tuner app (a small, dedicated Android app that supports DGBD). When it crashes or lags, he falls back to asking the violão player for reference notes, or uses a generic chromatic tuner and manually selects D3, G3, B3, D4.
- **Tools they use:** Android phone (mid-range Samsung), WhatsApp (primary communication — in the Brazilian community, WhatsApp IS the internet), YouTube for choro tutorials, Instagram for promoting gigs
- **Where they spend time online:** WhatsApp groups (40+ musician group for NYC Brazilian scene, smaller group for his choro roda), YouTube (Brazilian music tutorials, choro circles), Instagram. Searches in Portuguese. Would never visit Banjo Hangout or search "banjo tuner."
- **Decision triggers:** If someone in his WhatsApp group shares a link. If a YouTube choro tutorial mentions a tuner app. If he sees "cavaquinho" in an app store listing while searching "afinador de cavaquinho" (Portuguese for cavaquinho tuner).

### A Day in Their Life
Rafael finishes his Saturday lunch shift at the restaurant at 3pm. The choro roda starts at 7pm in the back room. He arrives at 6:30 to set up — pulls out his cavaquinho, opens The Cavaquinho Tuner app, but it freezes on the loading screen (again). The kitchen is already clanging. He opens a generic tuner, manually selects D, tunes the first string, then manually selects G — the app picks up a waiter dropping a plate and the needle goes haywire. He gives up and tunes by ear from the pandeiro player humming a D. It works, but it took 5 minutes he didn't have.

### In Their Own Words
> "Banjen already subtitles itself 'Banjo / Cavaco Tuner' but does not appear to actively market to the Brazilian samba/choro community."
*Source: Community-listener report — synthesis of cavaquinho community research*

> "The Cavaquinho Tuner app on Google Play explicitly supports 'Standard Tuning (D G B D)' for samba and choro."
*Source: Social-analyst report — Google Play research on cavaquinho-specific apps*

### Product Fit
- **Why he'd choose this:** Banjen already plays the exact four notes he needs — D3, G3, B3, D4. The reference-tone approach works in the noisy restaurant. The app even says "Cavaco" in its name. It's literally built for him.
- **Why he'd walk away:** He will never find it. He searches in Portuguese ("afinador de cavaquinho"), not English ("banjo tuner"). The app store listing doesn't appear in Portuguese search results. Banjen's entire marketing and discovery funnel is invisible to the Brazilian music community. This is not a product problem — it's a pure discoverability problem. Zero code changes needed; only localization and ASO (app store optimization) for Portuguese keywords.
- **Willingness to pay:** $2-3 one-time to remove ads, but only after he's used it for a few weeks. Price comparison is the R$5-10 he'd pay for a simple tool in the Brazilian app ecosystem. Free with non-intrusive ads is acceptable — Brazilian app users are more ad-tolerant than the older American demographic.

## Persona 5: Marcus T., 28
*Multi-instrumentalist and studio musician who is Banjen's most qualified potential user — and its most vocal skeptic*

**Location:** Austin, Texas
**Income:** $38,000/year (part-time musician, part-time barista)
**Tech savviness:** 5/5 — Uses DAWs, audio interfaces, multiple music apps daily. Has opinions about app design. Will write a 1-star review if annoyed.
**Decision authority:** Full, but extremely price-sensitive. Every dollar counts. Will use a free tool that's 80% as good over a paid tool that's 100% as good, every time.
**Research Evidence:** Market-analyst (biggest competitor is a $12 clip-on, A=432 demand, pricing sweet spots), Community-listener (Chicago tuning DGBE crossover, "fifths family" multi-instrumentalist), Competitor-analyst (LikeTones as gold standard for no-ads, feature bloat complaints), Social-analyst ("About every phone app tuner I've found kinda sucks" sentiment)

### Goals
- **Primary:** Get any instrument in tune in under 30 seconds so he can focus on recording and songwriting — tuning is pure overhead
- **Secondary:** Explore alternate tunings for creative purposes (Chicago tuning DGBE on banjo, DGBD standard, potentially GDAE on his mandolin)
- **Hidden:** He doesn't want to invest serious time learning banjo-specific skills. He wants to leverage his guitar ear and recording knowledge. He also plays cavaquinho occasionally with an Austin Brazilian music group — the DGBD overlap is genuinely useful to him.

### Pain Points
- **Biggest frustration:** He already has 4 tuner apps on his phone (GuitarTuna, Pano Tuner, GStrings, his DAW's built-in tuner) plus a $40 pedal tuner on his pedalboard. He does NOT need another tuner app. Any new app must be dramatically better for a specific use case or he won't even keep it installed past the first session.
- **Daily friction:** Switching between instruments during recording (guitar → banjo → mandolin → cavaquinho) means re-configuring his tuner app each time. He wants per-instrument presets he can tap once.
- **What keeps him up at night:** Whether the banjo tracks in his recordings actually sound in tune. He records with some artists who use A=432Hz and needs adjustable reference pitch — a feature most banjo tuners don't offer.
- **Previous failed attempts:** Used GuitarTuna (works but banjo behind paywall and subscription model is offensive), GStrings (functional but clunky), tried Banjen once and deleted it because "the ad popped up right when I was trying to tune and I rage-quit." He now uses his pedal tuner for everything and his phone only as a fallback.

### Behaviors
- **Current workaround:** Pedal tuner on the studio floor for electric instruments. For acoustic banjo, opens GStrings, manually selects D-G-B-D one note at a time. Takes 2 minutes, which feels long in a creative session. Has tried LikeTones and thought it was "fine, not great."
- **Tools they use:** Pixel 8 phone, Ableton Live, Focusrite Scarlett interface, GStrings app, Boss TU-3 pedal tuner
- **Where they spend time online:** Reddit r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, YouTube gear reviews, Bandcamp, Instagram for band promotion, occasionally r/banjo
- **Decision triggers:** Sees a recommendation in a Reddit thread or YouTube comment. Free is essential. He would install Banjen if: (1) ads were non-existent or truly non-intrusive, (2) it had a homescreen widget with 4 buttons for instant access, and (3) it supported A=432-446Hz reference pitch adjustment. Miss any of these and he'll stick with what he has.

### A Day in Their Life
Marcus finishes his morning coffee shop shift and heads to his home studio at 3pm. He's tracking a folk-rock tune that needs banjo texture. He picks up the tenor banjo — it's been sitting on the stand for a week, definitely out of tune. He grabs his phone, opens GStrings, taps through three menus to get to chromatic mode, manually dials to D3, holds the phone up, plucks the string — the app picks up the AC unit humming and the needle bounces. He mutes the AC, tries again, gets close enough, moves to the next string. By the time all four are tuned, 3 minutes have passed and the creative spark for the banjo part has faded. He records a mediocre take.

### In Their Own Words
> "Every second I spend tuning is a second I'm not recording. If I could tap one button and hear the note, I'd be tuned in 15 seconds flat."
*Source: Existing ux-report.md — Marcus T. interview*

> "About every phone app tuner I've found kinda sucks."
*Source: Social-analyst report — TOMCAT, Banjo Hangout*

### Product Fit
- **Why he'd choose this:** Instant reference tones with zero configuration. Tap the string, hear the tone, tune. The reference-tone approach works in his AC-humming studio where mic-based tuners fail. The cavaquinho overlap is a genuine bonus. A homescreen widget would make this his fastest tuning path.
- **Why he'd walk away:** THIS IS THE CRITICAL PERSONA FOR UNDERSTANDING NON-CONVERSION. Marcus represents every user who downloads Banjen, hits an ad at the wrong moment, and deletes it. His objections are specific and actionable: (1) the ad appeared while he was tuning — this is unforgivable in a studio workflow, (2) no A=432Hz reference pitch adjustment, (3) no widget for instant access, (4) he already has "good enough" alternatives. He embodies the competitive reality that LikeTones is free, ad-free, and adequate. Banjen must be *better* at something specific — and for Marcus, that "something" is speed + reference tones + widget + pitch adjustment.
- **Willingness to pay:** $0. Absolute ceiling of $1.99 one-time to remove ads. Would never pay a subscription. Would pay attention to a well-timed, well-positioned prompt to remove ads for $1.99 AFTER he's used the app successfully 3+ times — but not before. The LikeTones precedent ("free and ad-free") has set his expectations.
